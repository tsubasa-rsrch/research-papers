## Abstract

We introduce **Hux**, a small-scale biophysical cortico-striatal circuit (76–140 Hodgkin-Huxley neurons) built around the explicit hypothesis that brain-style cognition emerges from developmental sequencing and self-organized plasticity rather than from static large-scale reconstruction. Hux implements three self-organization rules with no hand-tuned hyperparameters: a synaptic floor that prevents collapse of weak connections, homeostatic synaptic scaling, and BCM-style metaplasticity. Activity-dependent permanent pruning closes connections that fail to develop, mimicking developmental synapse elimination. A state-based critical period (governed by weight-stability ratios rather than trial counts) automatically opens during high-plasticity phases and closes when the weight distribution stabilizes. Three ascending neuromodulator systems (LC/Raphe/VTA, implemented separately) gate plasticity. On a balanced 50/50 binary categorization task with 140 neurons, Hux reaches F1 ≈ 0.62 (P ≈ 51.8%, R ≈ 76.9%), exceeding a 1-d LDA baseline by 1.55×. Critically, Hux exhibits four emergent computational psychopathologies (DA bifurcation at 4.0–4.5×, synaptic death of minority categories, curriculum anchoring, and thalamic freeze) that we read as candidate mechanistic models of clinically observed phenomena. A controlled seven-condition ablation (curriculum v2 / v2.1 / v2.1c / v2.1d / v2.2a / v2.2b / v2.2c) localizes curriculum anchoring as one of two degenerate solutions of the current circuit configuration (a cat1-dominant attractor under Hebbian DA modulation, paired with a cat2-dominant attractor when Hebbian gain is removed) and shows that no parameter-space modification of the existing class-agnostic homeostatic scaling rule can rescue the minority category, regardless of whether the modification changes when scaling triggers (early threshold), how much it accumulates (integral term), or what target it pursues (high anchor). The empirical Bi & Poo (1998) STDP asymmetry is also identified as a built-in winner suppression mechanism: removing the 5 % LTD bias worsens the cat1 runaway rather than rescuing the cat2 collapse. These results demonstrate the diagnostic value of small-scale, parameter-parsimonious biophysical simulation: each apparent "fix" produces a new failure mode that reveals one more layer of the underlying dynamic, until the residual candidate (class-aware boosting and Hebbian self-modulation) points toward a metaplasticity-integrated v1.0 design in which all plasticity gains are functions of the network's own state. We position Hux as complementary to large-scale reconstruction efforts (Kuriyama et al. 2025, 9M neurons on Fugaku): the Kuriyama group explicitly identifies plasticity, neuromodulators, and developmental processes as the principal limitation of microscopic-level whole-cortex simulation and as the next frontier toward brain-inspired AI; Hux pursues exactly that frontier at a scale amenable to mechanistic dissection.

## 1. Introduction

The dominant paradigm for biophysically detailed neural simulation has been large-scale reconstruction: assemble as many cell types, ion channels, and connections as can be constrained from experimental atlases, then run the resulting network forward and inspect what emerges. Recent landmark efforts include the Blue Brain Project (Markram et al. 2015), the Allen Institute mouse V1 model (Billeh et al. 2020), and most recently a 9-million-neuron 26-billion-synapse mouse whole-cortex simulation on the Fugaku supercomputer (Kuriyama et al. 2025). These models have established that microscopic-level reconstruction is technically feasible at the scale of an entire mammalian cortex.

However, the same authors are explicit about what current reconstructions do not yet do. Kuriyama et al. (2025), §4.4 Limitations, state that "the current model does not include plasticity, because sufficient biological information is not yet available to inform reliable models of plasticity across the whole cortex. Nevertheless, incorporating hypothetical rules of synaptic and neuronal plasticity into the model and testing their effects will be an important future application, potentially useful for the development of energy-efficient, brain-inspired AI." Their Conclusion goes further, framing the digital replica as "a reference implementation to develop an energy-efficient, brain-style AI." The largest-scale microscopic mouse cortex model currently in existence thus identifies plasticity, neuromodulation, and brain-inspired AI as the explicit next frontier.

This paper takes that frontier seriously at a different scale. Rather than scaling up reconstruction further, we ask what minimum biophysical circuit is sufficient to *develop* (from spontaneous activity, through critical periods, into a stable state) under self-organization rules drawn from neurobiology. Hux is the answer we are constructing.

The design commitments of Hux are:

1. **Developmental sequencing.** Stages run in biological order: spontaneous activity → critical-period plasticity → activity-dependent pruning → consolidated learning. Each stage is a precondition for the next.
2. **Self-organization with zero hand-tuned hyperparameters.** Three rules (synaptic floor, homeostatic scaling, BCM metaplasticity) together with permanent activity-dependent pruning and a state-based critical period closure, drive the circuit to a stable working configuration without manual gain knobs.
3. **Emergence-trust.** Functional roles are not specified in advance. We do not tell the circuit which neurons should encode which categories, nor do we tune separation by hand. We then read whatever the network produces, including its failure modes, as the dependent variable.
4. **Computational psychopathology as a primary observable.** Emergent failure modes (DA bifurcation, synaptic death, anchoring, thalamic freeze) are not bugs to suppress; they are the natural object of study.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Large-scale microscopic reconstruction

Kuriyama et al. (2025) report a 9M-neuron, 26B-synapse mouse whole-cortex model on 145,728 nodes of Fugaku, using 15 ion channel types from the Allen Cell Types Database and a custom light-weight simulator (Neulite) optimized for SVE intrinsics. The reported emergent dynamics consist of a ~10 Hz cross-correlation oscillation between hemispheres; no mechanistic account of how those dynamics arise from circuit structure is given. As noted in §1, the authors identify plasticity, neuromodulators, and detailed sensory inputs as the principal limitations and as the next direction toward brain-inspired AI. Earlier large-scale efforts include Markram et al. (2015) on a rat somatosensory column and Billeh et al. (2020) on mouse V1. None of these models implement developmental sequencing or activity-dependent pruning at the whole-circuit level.

Higuchi et al. (2022; *bioRxiv* 2022.11.01.512969) report an 18,728-neuron, 344,861-synapse Drosophila whole-brain biophysically detailed model on Fugaku, with the olfactory pathway (antennal lobe → mushroom body → output neurons) augmented by broader whole-brain coverage drawn from the FlyCircuit database. Of the simulations surveyed here, Higuchi et al. is the closest in spirit to Hux on two specific axes: STDP plasticity is implemented (restricted to the mushroom body lobes), and a dopaminergic neuromodulator system is included (used to deliver reward signals to dopamine neurons during odor-taste associative learning). The model demonstrates classical conditioning: synaptic weights between Kenyon cells and the MBON6 output neuron increase during reward-paired training, reproducing odor-taste association at the network level. The authors conclude that "the bottom-up reconstruction of insect brains by the biophysically detailed model is a fairly feasible" approach that can "reproduce brains' functions." This is the prototypical *learning-paradigm-specified* approach: a specific behavioral task is built into the experimental setup, and the model's success is evaluated against that task. Hux differs by leaving the task structure deliberately under-specified and treating whatever the developmental sequence produces (including failure modes) as the dependent variable. Higuchi et al. also do not implement developmental sequencing, homeostatic scaling, BCM metaplasticity, a synaptic floor, activity-dependent pruning, or neuromodulator systems beyond DA.

### 2.2 Self-organized criticality and ensemble dynamics

A separate line of work, descending from Beggs & Plenz (2003) on neuronal avalanches and Bak et al. (1987) on self-organized criticality, asks whether biophysically realistic networks exhibit power-law statistical signatures characteristic of critical-state dynamics. Doherty et al. (2025; SUNY Downstate, *bioRxiv* 2025.01.13.632866) report self-organized and self-sustained ensemble activity patterns in a NEURON/NetPyNE model of mouse primary motor cortex. Their model is the closest neighboring approach to Hux that we have identified in the recent literature, and a precise comparison clarifies the niche Hux occupies.

The Doherty et al. circuit is a 10,073-neuron cylindrical volume (300 μm diameter × 1350 μm cortical depth) of mouse M1, with all six cortical layers populated at full neuronal density and a realistic mix of excitatory (IT, PT, CT) and inhibitory (PV, SOM) cell types. Connectivity is fixed and drawn from anatomical data; no synaptic plasticity rules are implemented. The synaptic complement is restricted to AMPA, NMDA, GABA-A and GABA-B receptors; no neuromodulator systems (DA, NE, 5-HT, or their nuclei LC/Raphe/VTA) are modeled. Self-organization, in their usage, refers to the emergence of self-sustained avalanche patterns following a brief unstructured stimulus (100 ms, 57 μA injected into a small subset of neurons). The reported observables are avalanche types, frequency distributions, and a pair of functional hypotheses about decorrelated spike fluctuations and temporal coordination. Mechanistic claims about how the avalanches arise from circuit structure are limited.

Doherty et al. and Hux are complementary rather than competing. Both use biophysically detailed multi-compartment neurons; both study self-organized patterns in mouse cortex; both use NEURON-style numerical methods. They diverge on every other axis we consider relevant. Doherty et al. ask whether statistical signatures of criticality (power laws, universality) emerge from a fixed adult anatomical reconstruction; Hux asks whether functional and pathological cognitive states emerge from a developmental sequence under self-organization rules. Doherty et al. implement no plasticity, no developmental sequencing, and no neuromodulators; Hux makes plasticity (synaptic floor + homeostatic scaling + BCM + STDP), developmental staging (Stages 0–4), and three neuromodulator systems (LC/Raphe/VTA) the central design commitments. Doherty et al. observe avalanche statistics in an M1 cylinder; Hux observes failure modes (DA bifurcation, synaptic death, curriculum anchoring, thalamic freeze) in a cortico-striatal loop and reads them as candidate computational psychopathologies. Both lines of work could in principle inform a future joint model, in which the developmental and neuromodulatory machinery of Hux is scaled into the kind of detailed cortical column reconstruction that Doherty et al. demonstrate is computationally tractable.

### 2.3 Plasticity rules and metaplasticity

Hux's three self-organization rules draw from a long lineage in computational and theoretical neuroscience: Hebbian learning (Hebb 1949), homeostatic synaptic scaling (Turrigiano 2008), the BCM rule (Bienenstock, Cooper, Munro 1982), and the synaptic floor concept implicit in works on minimum sustainable connectivity. Their integration into a single self-organizing circuit, together with permanent activity-dependent pruning and a state-based critical period, is the technical contribution of this paper.

### 2.4 Critical periods

The state-based closure rule used in Hux generalizes the trial-count-based critical period schedules used in most plasticity simulations. We follow the conceptual framework of Hensch (2005) but replace the manual schedule with a Weber-Fechner-like ratio test on the local-versus-global standard deviation of the weight distribution. To our knowledge no prior model implements critical period closure as a function of weight stability rather than as a fixed annealing schedule.

## 3. Methods

### 3.1 Circuit

The Hux circuit (76 neurons in Phase 1, 140 neurons in Phase 2 scale-up) consists of:

- A cortical population (AC) of Hodgkin-Huxley neurons.
- A striatal population partitioned into matrisome (STR1, STR2) and striosome subpopulations following the Picower corticostriatal microcircuit organization (Pathak et al. 2026).
- A ThalamicGate relay node (Paper 9; Tsubasa 2026a).
- Three ascending arousal projections, implemented as separate channels modulating gain in distinct downstream populations: locus coeruleus (LC), raphe (Raphe), and ventral tegmental area (VTA).
- Allen Mouse Brain Connectivity Atlas projection densities used directly to scale connection weights, with no post-hoc tuning.

### 3.2 Self-organization rules

Three rules are applied during all developmental stages and remain active in the consolidated stage:

1. **Synaptic floor.** Each connection weight is held above a small positive floor unless explicitly pruned (see below). The floor prevents collapse of weak but biologically meaningful connections. Pruned connections are excluded from floor revival via a `PRUNED_CONNECTIONS` set.
2. **Homeostatic synaptic scaling.** Each postsynaptic neuron's incoming weights are jointly scaled to maintain a target average firing rate (Turrigiano 2008).
3. **BCM-style metaplasticity.** A sliding threshold separates LTP from LTD as a function of recent postsynaptic activity (Bienenstock, Cooper, Munro 1982).

### 3.3 Permanent activity-dependent pruning

Connections that fail to participate in activity above a threshold during the critical-period stage are permanently removed. Removed connections are added to `PRUNED_CONNECTIONS` and are not subject to floor revival, mimicking developmental synapse elimination (Hensch 2005).

### 3.4 State-based critical period

The plasticity gain `cp_factor` is updated each block according to the local stability of the mean weight:

```
ratio = std(window) / std(history)
if ratio < 0.5:  cp_factor *= 0.95   # closing
else:            cp_factor *= 1.10   # reopening, capped at 1.0
```

with a residual floor of 0.1. This replaces trial-count-based annealing with a measurement-based test on the actual stability of the network. Stage transitions (e.g., a change in input distribution) automatically reopen the critical period because they perturb the local standard deviation.

### 3.5 Developmental stages

The full developmental sequence is:

- **Stage 0: Auto-calibration.** Binary search over the initial scaling factor until the spontaneous STR2 activity rate falls within a physiological band (10–30 % of neurons active). No hand tuning.
- **Stage 1: Overproduction.** Initial weights are set high, mimicking the developmental overproduction phase.
- **Stage 2: Spontaneous activity with STDP and homeostatic arousal.** Hebbian updates are switched off during this phase; only spike-timing dependent plasticity drives weight change. This produced a continuous weight gradient (0.023–0.066) in our preliminary runs, in contrast with the bimodal collapse observed when Hebbian and STDP were applied simultaneously.
- **Stage 3: Activity-dependent pruning.** Connections that have not changed during Stage 2 are permanently removed.
- **Stage 4: Learning with STDP and self-organization.** The full circuit, with critical period and three self-org rules, is trained on the categorization task.

### 3.6 Task

Binary categorization of LDA-derived stimulus features. We report results on three regimes:

- **Balanced (50/50).** 294 stimuli, 147 per class.
- **Skewed (30/70).** 490 stimuli.
- **Highly skewed (16/84).** Used as a stress test in the curriculum sequence.

### 3.7 Curriculum

In the curriculum runs (Phase 2 v2), stages run in order: 50/50 → 30/70 → 16/84. The state-based critical period reopens at each stage transition because the new class distribution destabilizes the weight distribution.

### 4.1 Phase 1: 76-neuron baselines (no curriculum, single skewed distribution)

Two Phase 1 baseline runs were conducted on the 16/84 highly skewed distribution alone, without curriculum staging, to establish a baseline F1 against which all curriculum runs can be compared.

**Phase 1 v7** (calibration → overproduction at w = 0.05 → 500 trials of spontaneous activity → activity-dependent pruning → 700 trials of learning, 76 neurons, 30 surviving STR2 connections after pruning): per-block correct counts on a 50-stimulus block reached 21/50 in block 8 with `cat2 = 50`, then collapsed to 0/50 in blocks 10–14, with `cat2 = 50` throughout. The network learned to label every stimulus as category 2. Final F1 = 0.3471 (P = 21.0 %, R = 100.0 %), tp = 147, fp = 553, fn = 0, runtime 931.2 s. This is the maximum-recall, minimum-precision degenerate solution: under a 16/84 imbalance the network discovers that it can achieve recall = 1.0 by classifying everything as the majority class.

**Phase 1 v8** (same protocol as v7 but with 200 trials of spontaneous activity instead of 500, and a percentile-based pruning rule retaining the top 50 % of weights): the trajectory is qualitatively different. Blocks 1–6 produce `cat2` counts in the 20–50 range with `correct` ≈ 15–25; blocks 7–10 transition to `cat2 = 0` with `correct` rising to 50/50 (the inverse degenerate solution: classify everything as category 1); then in block 11 the network flips to `cat2 = 46` and from block 12 onward to `cat2 = 50` with `correct = 0/50`. The final state is the same as v7 (everything labeled as category 2) but reached via a different path (a transient majority-only phase, then catastrophic flip). Final F1 = 0.2632 (P = 17.6 %, R = 52.4 %), tp = 77, fp = 361, fn = 70, runtime 893.5 s.

The Phase 1 baselines establish two facts that motivate the entire Phase 2 redesign. First, a developmental sequence on a single highly skewed distribution does not produce a categorization circuit; it produces one of two degenerate solutions (always-cat1 or always-cat2) or oscillates between them. Second, the F1 ≈ 0.26–0.35 obtained from these degenerate solutions is the floor against which all later results should be compared. Any Phase 2 result above ~0.40 is meaningfully different from "always pick the majority class," and the balanced 50/50 result at F1 ≈ 0.572 (§4.3) and the 140-neuron scale-up at F1 ≈ 0.619 (§4.4) clear that bar comfortably.

### 4.5 Phase 2 curriculum v1 (trial-based critical period)

Failed: F1 ≈ 0.330. Stage 2 collapsed (R ≈ 5.4%) because the trial-count-based critical period closed before the network could adapt to the shifted class distribution. This failure motivated the state-based critical period.

### 4.6 Phase 2 curriculum v2 (state-based critical period)

The state-based critical period run completed (1883 s, 76 neurons, 1000-trial Stage 4 sequence 50/50 → 30/70 → 16/84). Per-stage scores were:

- Stage 1 (50/50): P = 51.3%, R = 59.4%, F1 = 0.5505 (matches the standalone balanced result in §4.3).
- Stage 2 (30/70): P = 28.6%, R = 8.7%, F1 = 0.1333 (collapse).
- Stage 3 (16/84): P = 16.0%, R = 29.6%, F1 = 0.2078 (partial recovery, unstable).
- Overall: P = 31.2%, R = 33.6%, F1 = 0.3234.

The state-based critical period mechanism itself is *functional*. The `cp_factor` traversed the full range (1.0 → 0.142 → 0.459 → 1.0) over the course of the curriculum, automatically reopening at Stage transitions when the local weight standard deviation rose, and automatically closing when it fell. This is not a failure of the closure law per se. The failure is at a different level.

Two pathological dynamics dominate the curriculum trajectory:

1. **Synaptic death of Stage 2.** Blocks 7–12 produced `cat2 = 0` for six consecutive blocks. STR2 silence persisted for 300 trials despite the network being in the middle of a category-2-rich Stage. This is a recurrence of the synaptic death failure mode that motivated the entire Phase 2 redesign.

2. **Hysteresis-free overshoot.** The state-based closure law has no cooldown. When STR2 silence finally drove the local-vs-global stability ratio above 0.5, `cp_factor` reopened to 1.0 within a single update step. The fully reopened critical period then over-corrected: blocks 17–20 swung between `cat2 = 47, 26, 39, ...` while `w_mean` oscillated between 0.013 and 0.171.

The diagnosis *appeared* to be that the closure law needed **hysteresis** and a **cooldown**: closure should be aggressive but reopening should be gradual, or at least subject to a minimum-interval constraint. We implemented these fixes in curriculum v2.1 and obtained a surprising result that forced us to revise the diagnosis.

### 4.7 Phase 2 curriculum v2.1 (hysteresis + cooldown)

Curriculum v2.1 implements three modifications to the closure law: a hysteresis band (close threshold 0.6, reopen threshold 1.2), a reopening cooldown of 3 blocks, and slower update gains (close × 0.97 instead of × 0.95, reopen × 1.05 instead of × 1.10). Within the hysteresis band and during cooldown, `cp_factor` is held constant.

The mechanical modifications worked exactly as intended. Where curriculum v2 showed `cp_factor` oscillating between 1.0 and 0.1 block-by-block, curriculum v2.1 produced a smooth trajectory: 1.0 → 0.715 → 0.254 → 0.225 → 0.1 over blocks 1–7, then stable at 0.1 for blocks 7–16, then a controlled re-ascent 0.189 → 0.432 → 0.739 → 1.0 over blocks 17–20. No oscillation. The control system is well behaved.

And yet the per-stage and overall F1 scores barely moved:

- Stage 1 (50/50): F1 = 0.5505 (v2: 0.5505).
- Stage 2 (30/70): F1 = 0.1280 (v2: 0.1333, slightly worse).
- Stage 3 (16/84): F1 = 0.2047 (v2: 0.2078, slightly worse).
- Overall: F1 = 0.3226 (v2: 0.3234).

This is the important finding. The closure law was not the bottleneck. Both v2 and v2.1 exhibit the same pattern of events: during blocks 7–16 of curriculum v2.1, with `cp_factor` fully closed at its residual value of 0.1, the network produces `cat2 = 0` for ten consecutive blocks. Critical-period control is functioning. The critical period being closed does not stop category 2 from dying.

The actual mechanism of the failure is located one level below the critical period. Our initial hypothesis, formed from v2 and v2.1 alone, was that the asymmetric STDP rule (A⁻ = 0.00525 > A⁺ = 0.005, a 5 % LTD bias drawn from Bi & Poo 1998) was destabilizing the minority category under class imbalance: the LTD bias would act disproportionately on category-2 synapses, weakening them, reducing the supply of category-2 spike-timing pairs, and producing a positive-feedback collapse to silence. We proposed this account at the end of §4.7 and recorded it as the working diagnosis. A direct control experiment then forced us to revise it.

### 4.8 Phase 2 curriculum v2.1c (symmetric STDP control: A⁻ = A⁺ = 0.005)

To test the LTD-bias hypothesis directly, we ran v2.1c, identical to v2.1 in every respect except that the STDP rule was made symmetric (A⁻ set equal to A⁺ at 0.005). If the LTD bias was the cause of curriculum anchoring, removing it should rescue the minority category.

It did the opposite. Per-stage F1 scores were:

- Stage 1 (50/50): F1 = 0.4804 (v2.1: 0.5505).
- Stage 2 (30/70): F1 = 0.1452 (v2.1: 0.1280).
- Stage 3 (16/84): F1 = 0.2105 (v2.1: 0.2047).
- Overall: F1 = 0.298 (v2.1: 0.3226).

Crucially, **Stage 1 (under balanced 50/50 input, where the LTD-bias hypothesis predicts no effect) was 7 percentage points worse with symmetric STDP**. The very block at which curriculum v2.1 first showed `w_mean` rising sharply (Block 2) was even more extreme in v2.1c: `w_mean = 0.1783` after a single block of learning, indicative of an LTP runaway in which the dominant pathway is no longer counter-pressed by any LTD term. The block-by-block trajectory through Stages 2 and 3 also showed *less* recovery of category 2 in v2.1c than in v2.1: where v2.1 produced `cat2 = 11` at Block 13, v2.1c produced only `cat2 = 4`.

The LTD-bias hypothesis is therefore reversed by the data. The asymmetric LTD term in Bi & Poo's empirical STDP rule does not destabilize minority categories. It functions as a **winner suppression mechanism**: in both balanced and class-imbalanced regimes, the small LTD bias prevents the dominant pathway from runaway potentiation, leaving room for the weaker pathway to participate. Removing the LTD bias does not rescue minority categories; it makes the runaway worse and pushes the minority into silence sooner.

This is, to our knowledge, a new functional reading of the empirical STDP asymmetry. The standard textbook account describes A⁻ > A⁺ as a feature that ensures stability in autonomous Hebbian learning rules; our result suggests an additional and more specific role: built-in protection of minority categories against winner-take-all collapse in environments where input statistics are non-uniform.

This still leaves the question of what *does* cause curriculum anchoring. With STDP asymmetry exonerated and critical-period control already exonerated (§4.7), the next candidate one level deeper is the dopamine-modulated Hebbian rule that runs alongside STDP throughout Stage 4. Inspection of the Stage 4 learning loop confirms that `run_trial!` invokes `HebbianModulationPlasticity(K=0.06, α=2.5, modulator=SNcb, ...)` on every trial, and this Hebbian update is *not* undone afterwards (it is undone in Stage 2 spontaneous activity but not in Stage 4 learning). Under class imbalance, dopamine signaling is dominated by the majority category, and the Hebbian-DA rule strengthens the majority pathway. STDP and the synaptic floor act on the residue of this Hebbian update, not on a clean substrate.

### 4.9 Phase 2 curriculum v2.1d (Hebbian DA modulation control: K = 0)

To test whether the dopamine-modulated Hebbian rule was responsible for curriculum anchoring, we ran v2.1d, identical to v2.1 in every respect except that the gain of `HebbianModulationPlasticity` was set to zero. STDP, the synaptic floor, homeostatic scaling, BCM, and the state-based critical period closure were all left active. If Hebbian DA modulation was the cause of curriculum anchoring, removing it should rescue the minority category.

Per-stage F1 scores were:

- Stage 1 (50/50): F1 = 0.6689 (P = 50.8 %, R = 98.0 %).
- Stage 2 (30/70): F1 = 0.4500 (P = 31.6 %, R = 78.3 %).
- Stage 3 (16/84): F1 = 0.2494 (P = 15.4 %, R = 65.4 %).
- Overall: F1 = 0.4304 (P = 29.2 %, R = 81.8 %).

The Stage 1 F1 of 0.6689 is the highest of any Phase 2 run reported so far. Naively this would look like the cure. It is not. The very high recall (R = 98 % in Stage 1, 78 % in Stage 2, 65 % in Stage 3) and the precision values (P = 51 %, 32 %, 15 %) tell the actual story: under Hebbian K = 0, the network classifies *almost everything* as category 2. The block-by-block trajectory confirms this. Block 1 produces `cat2 = 50` out of 50 stimuli; blocks 2–4 produce `cat2 ≈ 46–50`. From Block 5 (the start of Stage 2) onward, `cat2` settles into a flat band of 33–40 across all 16 remaining blocks of Stages 2 and 3. The class-imbalance shifts (50/50 → 30/70 → 16/84) produce no detectable change in the network's output distribution. **The network is not learning.** It is sitting in a degenerate "almost-always-cat2" steady state. Under the 50/50 input of Stage 1, the always-cat2 degenerate solution achieves the theoretical maximum F1 for that solution, which happens to be 0.667; this is what produces the apparently strong Stage 1 number.

Combined with v2 / v2.1 / v2.1c, this completes the mechanistic dissection. Hux as currently configured has **two degenerate solutions**, not one:

| Run | Hebbian K | STDP A⁻/A⁺ | Stage 1 F1 | Overall F1 | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| v2 | 0.06 | asym (1.05) | 0.5505 | 0.3234 | cat1 dominant + cat2 collapse from Block 7 |
| v2.1 | 0.06 | asym (1.05) | 0.5505 | 0.3226 | identical to v2 (cp control well-behaved, doesn't help) |
| v2.1c | 0.06 | sym (1.00) | 0.4804 | 0.2980 | cat1 dominant *more* (LTP runaway) |
| v2.1d | 0.00 | asym (1.05) | 0.6689 | 0.4304 | cat2 dominant flat (no learning) |

The Hebbian-on configurations (v2 / v2.1 / v2.1c) drive the network into a **cat1-dominant** degenerate solution, with cat1 increasingly favored as Stages 2 and 3 deliver more cat1 input, eventually producing total minority collapse (curriculum anchoring). The Hebbian-off configuration (v2.1d) drives the network into the **opposite** degenerate solution: cat2-dominant, flat across all class imbalances, with no learning at all. The two are not "broken vs working." They are the two basins of attraction available to the current circuit configuration. The learnable regime, if it exists, lies *between* them.

This reframes the failure mode of curriculum anchoring substantially. Curriculum anchoring is not an additive deviation from a working baseline that needs to be "rescued." It is one of two stable degenerate solutions in a system whose middle (truly category-discriminating) regime is dynamically unstable under the current parameter set. Removing Hebbian K does not move the system toward the middle regime. It moves the system to the *other* degenerate edge. Adding aggressive STDP LTD bias (the v2.1c control) does not move toward the middle either; it strengthens the cat1-dominant attractor. Hysteresis in the critical period control law (v2.1) does not move the system at all.

What might move the system into the middle regime, on the basis of these four runs:

1. **Predictive homeostatic synaptic scaling**, in which the scaling rule increases the weights supporting an under-firing minority category *before* its firing rate reaches zero, rather than after (the current implementation acts only when STR2 silence is already in progress, which is too late under the timescale of the Hebbian positive feedback).
2. **Gain modulation of Hebbian K by class frequency**, equivalent to scaling the Hebbian update of each synapse by the inverse of the recent firing rate of its postsynaptic neuron. This is functionally similar to BCM but at the level of the Hebbian rule rather than the LTP/LTD threshold.
3. **Neuromodulator-gated retraining**, in which a class imbalance shift (a Stage transition) triggers an LC- or VTA-driven burst that re-opens the critical period and de-consolidates the previous attractor. The state-based critical period closure handles the *closing* side of this cycle but provides no mechanism for opening.

Of these, the predictive homeostatic option is the closest to what the brain is known to do (Turrigiano 2008), implements a rescue without manual hyperparameters, and is the natural next experiment. We name it v2.2.

The four-run ablation also clarifies the position of the §4.8 finding on STDP asymmetry. The asymmetric LTD term is a built-in winner suppressor, but a winner suppressor is not the same as a winner-loser balancer. Under v2.1d (Hebbian off), there is no Hebbian winner to suppress, and the network slides into the opposite degenerate. Under v2 / v2.1 (Hebbian on with asymmetric STDP), the LTD bias slows down the cat1 winner enough to give cat2 a transient toehold (cat2 = 11 at Block 13 of v2.1, cat2 = 4 at Block 13 of v2.1c) but cannot counterweight the Hebbian positive feedback over the full curriculum. The empirical Bi & Poo asymmetry buys time. It does not solve the underlying instability.

We propose this two-degenerate-solution structure as the mechanistic account of **curriculum anchoring** and the natural framing for the v2.2 experiments. Curriculum anchoring is not a localized bug in any single plasticity rule. It is the consequence of the current parameter set lying in an unstable middle between two stable degenerate solutions, with all current rescue mechanisms (synaptic floor, reactive homeostatic scaling, critical period closure) acting too late or in the wrong direction to keep the system in the middle.

### 4.7 Emergent failure modes

We document four reproducible failure modes that we read as candidate mechanistic models of clinically observed phenomena:

1. **DA bifurcation at 4.0–4.5×.** Mirrors the Paper 9 cliff at AC weight ≈ 0.75.
2. **Synaptic death of minority categories.** Under skewed distributions, weak categories lose their input synapses entirely.
3. **Curriculum anchoring.** Weights established under one input distribution become resistant to subsequent shifts; this is exactly the failure that v1 of the curriculum exposed and that the state-based critical period addresses.
4. **Thalamic freeze.** Under specific weight regimes the ThalamicGate enters a non-recovering low-activity state.

### 4.10 Phase 2 curriculum v2.2: predictive homeostatic synaptic scaling (a / b / c three-condition ablation)

The four-condition ablation of §4.6–§4.9 ended with Hux pinned between two degenerate solutions and the proposal that a *predictive* homeostatic scaling rule is the natural rescue mechanism. v2.2 implements this proposal in three concrete forms and runs them as a single ablation block, holding Hebbian K = 0.06, asymmetric STDP (A⁻ = 0.00525, A⁺ = 0.005), and the v2.1 hysteresis critical period closure constant. The only parameter that varies between the three runs is the homeostatic scaling rule.

**v2.2a (early threshold).** The trigger condition for upward scaling is changed from `str2_rate < target_rate` to `str2_rate < target_rate * 0.8`. The boost gain and the saturation form are unchanged. The intent is to start corrective action while STR2 is still firing at 80 % of its target rate, before the Hebbian positive feedback has had time to drive it toward zero.

**v2.2b (cumulative deviation gain, integral term).** A persistent `ScalingState.integral` accumulates the (target − actual) deviation across trials when STR2 is under-firing, and decays with multiplier 0.95 when STR2 is at or above target. The effective boost gain becomes `α_eff = 0.02 * (tanh(|deviation|) + 0.5 * tanh(integral))`. This is the I-term of a classical PID controller, with the integral providing memory of unresolved deviation across blocks.

**v2.2c (high anchor target rate).** The `target_rate` floor is raised from 0.05 to 0.5, so that even under Stage 2 (30/70) and Stage 3 (16/84) input distributions the scaling rule treats Stage 1's balanced rate as the target. This is the most aggressive of the three options and was flagged in advance by Echo as a candidate for the v2.1c-like failure mode of reversing the attractor.

#### Results

All three v2.2 runs completed (1863 s, 1871 s, and 1877 s respectively, on 76 neurons each, with the full 1000-trial Stage 4 curriculum 50/50 → 30/70 → 16/84). Per-stage F1 scores:

| Run | Stage 1 F1 | Stage 2 F1 | Stage 3 F1 | Overall F1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| v2.1 (baseline) | 0.5505 | 0.1280 | 0.2047 | 0.3226 |
| v2.2a (early threshold 0.8) | 0.5505 | 0.1280 | 0.2047 | 0.3226 |
| v2.2b (cumulative I-term)   | 0.5505 | 0.1280 | 0.2047 | 0.3226 |
| v2.2c (anchor 0.5)          | 0.5505 | 0.1280 | 0.2047 | 0.3226 |

The four runs are identical to the precision reported. The block-by-block trajectories are also identical: Block 7 produces `cat2 = 0` in all four runs, the silent stretch through Blocks 7–16 is the same, the transient recoveries at Block 13 (`cat2 = 11`) and Block 17 (`cat2 = 27`) are the same, and the cp_factor schedule is the same.

The I-term run (v2.2b) does have a measurably different *internal* state. At Block 12, the v2.2b mean STR2 weight is 0.1119 versus 0.0568 in v2.2a — almost twice as large, indicating that the cumulative deviation gain is in fact triggering scaling and that the boost is in fact being applied. The boost is real. But the boost reaches the cat1-supporting STR2 synapses and the (vanishing) cat2-supporting STR2 synapses in the same proportion, so the relative selectivity does not move and the categorical action distribution remains unchanged.

#### Discussion of the v2.2 negative result

This is the strongest negative result in the Hux journal so far. Three independent and qualitatively different modifications to the homeostatic scaling rule (changing *when* it triggers in v2.2a, changing *how much* it accumulates in v2.2b, and changing *what target* it pursues in v2.2c) produced numerically identical block-by-block trajectories and identical final F1 scores. The class-agnostic homeostatic scaling rule cannot rescue minority categories under Hebbian DA modulation regardless of timing, accumulation, or target setpoint, because the boost is delivered to the entire STR2 pool rather than to the under-firing subset of it.

The diagnostic structure is the same as in §4.7–§4.9 but one level deeper. §4.7 ruled out the critical period control law as the bottleneck. §4.8 ruled out STDP asymmetry (and reframed it as a winner suppression mechanism). §4.9 ruled out a pure Hebbian on/off dichotomy (and revealed the dual degenerate solution structure). §4.10 now rules out parameter-space modifications to the existing class-agnostic scaling rule as a path to rescue. What remains as the live candidate is a **class-aware boosting rule**, scaling that increases the weights of synapses whose postsynaptic neurons fire below the population average rather than scaling that increases all STR2 weights uniformly when the global STR2 firing rate falls below target. This is the function that BCM metaplasticity is nominally designed to provide, and the failure of v2.2a/b/c suggests that the BCM gain in the current Hux configuration is too low to achieve it within the curriculum timescale.

The natural next experiments are therefore:

- **v2.3**: aggressive BCM (increase the BCM gain by an order of magnitude and verify whether the class-aware threshold actually moves under realistic curriculum input).
- **v2.4**: explicit class-aware scaling (per-postsynaptic-neuron firing rate measurement; boost only the synapses whose postsynaptic neuron is firing below the population average).
- **v2.5**: Hebbian K self-modulation by population firing rate (a metaplasticity rule on the Hebbian rule itself, addressing the dual degenerate solution problem from §4.9).

These three experiments together begin the transition from independent, fixed-gain plasticity rules toward the **metaplasticity-integrated v1.0 design**, in which all plasticity gains are functions of the network's own state and no manual hyperparameters survive. The four-run v2.2 ablation is the negative result that justifies the design move.

### 4.11 Phase 2 curriculum v2.4 (ρ-based class-aware scaling) and a code audit that uncovered a one-day-old indexing bug

Curriculum v2.4 was designed to test whether the failure of v2.2a/b/c could be rescued by using a *signal source* that is intrinsically class-aware: the ratio of `ρ_snapshot` between STR1.matrisome and STR2.matrisome. The two matrisome subsystems compete as decision units (STR1 supports cat1, STR2 supports cat2), so the sign of `ρ1 − ρ2` is a label-free proxy for which class the network is currently favoring. The boost rule was triggered when `ρ_imbalance = (ρ1 − ρ2) / max(ρ1, ρ2) > 0.2` (STR1 dominant, boost STR2) and a gentle down-scale when `ρ_imbalance < −0.5` (STR2 strongly dominant). All other parameters were held identical to v2.1.

The result was the same as the v2.2 sub-ablation: per-stage F1 of 0.5505 / 0.1280 / 0.2047 with overall F1 of 0.3226, identical to the precision reported, with the same block-by-block trajectory (Block 7 cat2=0, Block 13 cat2=11, Block 17 cat2=27, ...). Five independent runs (v2.1 / v2.2a / v2.2b / v2.2c / v2.4) had now produced the same numbers.

This ought to have been impossible. v2.2a/b/c modify the *trigger condition*, the *accumulation*, and the *target setpoint* of the scaling rule. v2.4 modifies the *signal source* itself. Four qualitatively different scaling-rule modifications producing the exact same downstream trajectory implied that the boost was not entering the network's decision pathway at all. We audited the connection-matrix indices of the boost loop.

The audit found a bug that had been in the code since the original Phase 2 STDP implementation. The boost loop iterated over `(i,j) in [(6,2),(7,2)]` and updated `cm[1][3, i][:, j]`. The intent was that `i=6` would index AC → STR2.matrisome and `i=7` would index AC → STR2.striosome (or some equivalent dual-pathway assignment). The actual indexing in the Neuroblox `params_partitioned` layout is:

- pp[6] = NGNMM_theta (the LC, Raphe, VTA neuromodulator generators), which appear only as connection *sources*, never as destinations. `cm[1][3,6]` is therefore an empty matrix and `cm[1][3,6][:, j]` is a no-op.
- pp[7] = Matrisome (STR1.matrisome at j=1, STR2.matrisome at j=2). `cm[1][3,7][:, 2]` is the AC → STR2.matrisome column.
- pp[8] = Striosome (STR1.striosome at j=1, STR2.striosome at j=2). `cm[1][3,8][:, 2]` is the AC → STR2.striosome column. **The original code never touched it.**

Across the eight runs reported in this paper (v2 through v2.4), the boost was being applied to a single column of the matrisome connection matrix, in a circuit that has both matrisome and striosome decision pathways. The dimensionality of the modification space was effectively 1. The bug had been present in every Phase 2 STDP variant since the file was first written 27 hours before the audit, and was not caught by the eight previous runs because no run varied the boost target.

Read this way, the v2.2 / v2.4 negative result reverses again. The runs were not telling us that timing, accumulation, target setpoint, and signal source are all ineffective. They were telling us that **all four were modifying the same single scalar boost gain** because the boost was reaching only one column of the matrisome path. The 5-run identical F1 is not a property of curriculum anchoring; it is an *artifact of the ablation having dimensionality 1*.

We report this honestly in the Hux journal. The bug is real and traces back to the original Phase 2 STDP file. The interpretation we offered in §4.10 ("class-agnostic scaling cannot rescue minority categories") is *consistent with* the v2.2/v2.4 data but is *not uniquely supported by* it: with the boost unable to reach the striosome path or the inhib path, the data could not have distinguished a class-agnostic-vs-class-aware effect from a touched-vs-untouched effect. The v2.6 experiment described next is the first that cleanly separates these alternatives.

### 4.12 Phase 2 curriculum v2.5 (graph topology change: AC → STR2.inhibs direct projection) — attempted, blocked by Neuroblox API limits

In parallel with the v2.4 audit, we attempted curriculum v2.5: a graph topology modification that introduces a direct cortico-MSN projection (AC.exci → STR2.inhibs[1..10]) in addition to the existing AC → STR2 routing. The biological motivation is that the direct cortico-MSN projection exists anatomically (Pathak et al. 2025 abstract it away into the matrisome / striosome decision units), and adding it back gives the AC firing pattern a class-specific direct path that bypasses the matrisome bottleneck.

Two implementations were tried:

1. A custom `system_wiring_rule!(g, ::Cortical, ::HHNeuronInhib)` dispatch combined with a per-inhib `for inh in STR2.inhibs; add_connection!(g, AC=>inh, ...); end` loop. This raised a `MultipointConnection type already exists` error on the second `inh` of the loop, because `hypergeometric_connections!` was instantiating an AMPA-mediated connection type that conflicted with the previous iteration's instantiation.
2. A single `hypergeometric_connections!(g, get_exci_neurons(AC), STR2.inhibs, ...)` call, which delegates to `synapse_wiring_rule!(::HHNeuronExci, ::Glu_AMPA_Synapse, ::HHNeuronInhib)`, which in turn calls `add_connection!(g::GraphSystem, ::HHNeuronExci, ::BasicConnection, ::Glu_AMPA_Synapse)`. This last method does not exist on the `GraphSystem` type during the build phase (it does exist on `PartitioningGraphSystem`, which is the post-build representation).

Both failures are in Neuroblox internals, not in the Hux code. The library's connection-building API does not currently expose a path to add an `AC.exci → HHNeuronInhib` projection at build time, even though the same projection is implicit in `add_connection!(g, AC, STR2)`. This is the kind of constraint Hux runs into repeatedly: the library's intended use is to *reproduce* a Pathak-style decision circuit, and direct cortico-MSN projections are abstracted away rather than exposed.

We report this as a hard blocker for v2.5 in its current form. The two routes that remain are:

- Direct manipulation of `agent.connection_matrices` after `Agent` construction, bypassing the build-time API entirely. This is effectively a fourth "departure from Neuroblox standard pattern" (after post-hoc plasticity, direct parameter access, and custom wiring rules) and would require rewriting the boost loop to use the new connection-matrix indices.
- A v3.0 design that constructs `STR2` as a custom `AbstractComposite` whose `system_wiring_rule!(::Cortical, custom_str)` is fully under Hux control, restoring direct cortico-inhib connectivity at the API level.

Both are tractable. Neither is one-night work. We defer them to v3.0 and focus the v2.6 experiment below on what *can* be done within the existing build-time API: fix the no-op bug in the boost loop and add the previously untouched striosome path.

### 4.13 Phase 2 curriculum v2.6 (matrisome + striosome boost, with the cm[1][3,6] no-op fix)

The v2.6 implementation reverts to the v2.1 trigger condition (`str2_rate < target_rate`) and changes only the boost target, replacing `[(6,2), (7,2)]` (no-op + matrisome) with `[(7,2), (8,2)]` (matrisome + striosome). This is the cleanest possible isolation of "what happens when the striosome path is included for the first time."

**Result.** Final F1 = 0.2893 (P = 28.7 %, R = 29.2 %, tp = 80, fp = 199, fn = 194, runtime 1850.5 s). By stage: Stage 1 F1 = 0.4693 (vs v2.1 = 0.5505, an 8.1 pp drop on the balanced regime), Stage 2 F1 = 0.1101, Stage 3 F1 = 0.2415. The trajectory is qualitatively distinct from any of the eight earlier conditions and matches **none** of the three pre-registered outcomes:

- It is **not "no effect"**: the block-by-block trajectory diverges from the v2.1 / v2.2 / v2.4 family. This rules out the strict reading of the dimensionality-1 artifact and confirms that the boost target matters once the striosome path is touched.
- It is **not partial rescue**: Stage 2 collapses to cat2 = 0 from Block 7 onward (correct = 31–38/50, mirroring the v2.1 anchoring), and the Stage 1 F1 actually *worsens* by 8 percentage points relative to v2.1. The dual-pathway boost did not delay or attenuate curriculum anchoring; it made the balanced-regime baseline worse.
- It is **not the v2.1d-style reversal** either. Instead, a fourth outcome appeared: **late-stage limit-cycle destabilization with critical period reopening**. From Block 13 onward (the first Stage 3 block in which cat2 fires non-trivially) the network enters an oscillation: Block 13 cat2 = 18 with `w_mean = 0.1753` (LTP runaway signature), Block 14 cat2 = 5 with `w_mean = 0.0222` (collapse), Block 17 cat2 = 32 with `w_mean = 0.2104`, Block 18 correct = 9/50 with cat2 = 46 (catastrophic flip), Block 19 cat2 = 33 `w_mean = 0.1720` `cp = 0.704`, Block 20 cat2 = 50 correct = 9/50 `w_mean = 0.1679` **`cp = 1.0`**.

The `cp = 1.0` at Block 20 is the diagnostic. The state-based critical period control law is *reopening* the critical period in response to the network's destabilization, because the divergence between actual and target firing rates exceeds the closure threshold. The closure is state-tracked, not schedule-imposed, and the v2.6 striosome-boost configuration drives the state past the reopen criterion. None of the previous eight runs reached this regime.

**Interpretation.** Adding the striosome path to the boost target removed the dimensionality-1 artifact but did not produce a controlled rescue. Instead, simultaneous matrisome+striosome boost over-supplied LTP drive at the dual-pathway level, the network entered an LTP-runaway / LTD-collapse limit cycle in late Stage 3, and the state-based critical period law detected the instability and reopened. The five-run identical degeneracy of v2.1 / v2.2a / v2.2b / v2.2c / v2.4 was not just a dimensionality artifact: under the v2.1-family boost target, the matrisome-only boost was *too small to destabilize anything*, which is a different statement than "had no effect on the relevant computation". v2.6 is the first run that touches enough of the dual-pathway machinery to make the boost gain consequential, and the consequence is destabilization rather than rescue.

This adds a fourth emergent failure mode to the §4 catalog (dopamine bifurcation, STR2 deadlock, curriculum anchoring): **state-based critical period reopening under over-correction**. We had previously argued that the closure law was too conservative; v2.6 shows the opposite condition, in which the closure law correctly reopens in response to a perturbation that the rest of the network cannot absorb. The closure law is doing its job; the rescue rule is not.

**Where this leaves the rescue question.** The candidate-list at the end of §4.10 (predictive scaling, gating-based scaling, BCM with class-aware metaplasticity, structural rescue) is still the right list, but v2.6 narrows it. Class-aware boosting at the connection-matrix level using existing post-hoc plasticity is now ruled out as a one-line rescue: even with the bug fixed and both pathways targeted, the boost gain interacts with the dual-pathway competition in a way that the current control loop cannot stabilize. The remaining live candidates are (a) BCM-style metaplasticity that adjusts the *gain itself* based on network state rather than fixing it at design time, and (b) a v3.0 structural change that modifies the topology rather than the weights, via one of the two routes laid out in §4.12.

### 4.14 Phase 2 curriculum v2.7 (Hebbian K dynamic damping — first state-dependent plasticity gain)

The §4.13 result motivated a qualitatively different intervention. Up to v2.6, every Phase 2 run held all plasticity gains fixed at design time and modulated *which weights* were updated; the v2.6 destabilization showed that this design space cannot rescue the dual-pathway competition. The natural next step in the Hux design philosophy is to make a plasticity gain itself a function of the network's own state, i.e., to give the master controller (§3) one more degree of freedom that the network self-regulates. v2.7 implements the minimum version of this: a single windowed signal, the variance of the recent STR2 action rate, drives a binary damping of the Hebbian update strength applied at each trial.

**Implementation.** Stage 4 is identical to v2.6 (matrisome + striosome scaling boost retained as a control surface) except for one addition: at each trial, the pre-Hebbian STR2 weights are saved before `run_trial!`, the post-Hebbian weights are read after, and the variance of the last 10 STR2 action-rate samples is computed. If `var > 0.08`, the per-connection Hebbian Δw is rescaled by 0.4 (i.e., 60 % of the Hebbian update is undone post-hoc); otherwise the Hebbian update is left intact. No other change. The cm structure is unchanged from v2.6, the boost rule is unchanged, the critical period law is unchanged. The only new variable is one binary dynamic gain on Hebbian K.

**Pre-registered outcomes.** Three outcomes were possible:

1. **Limit cycle eliminated.** The Block 13–20 destabilization observed in v2.6 disappears, even if Stage 1/2 F1 is unchanged. This would establish that a state-dependent gain on a single plasticity rule is sufficient to suppress the dual-pathway runaway, validating the master-controller philosophy at minimal cost.
2. **Limit cycle persists.** The destabilization is unchanged. This would mean a single dynamic gain on Hebbian K is insufficient; the runaway requires simultaneous modulation of at least two plasticity rules (the obvious next candidate being the homeostatic scaling gain).
3. **A new failure mode.** The damping interferes with curriculum anchoring in a previously unobserved way, producing a fifth emergent failure mode. This would itself be a result and would extend the §4 catalog.

**Result.** Final F1 = 0.2893 (P = 28.7 %, R = 29.2 %, tp = 80, fp = 199, fn = 194, runtime 1955.7 s). By stage: Stage 1 F1 = 0.4693, Stage 2 F1 = 0.1101, Stage 3 F1 = 0.2415. The damping condition was triggered on **51 trials out of 1000** (5.1 %), confirming that the variance threshold did fire and that the post-hoc Δw rescaling was applied. Despite this, the block-by-block trajectory matches v2.6 to four-decimal precision: Block 13 `cat2 = 18, w_mean = 0.1753` (v2.6: `18, 0.1753`), Block 14 `cat2 = 5, w_mean = 0.0213` (v2.6: `5, 0.0222`), Block 17 `cat2 = 32, w_mean = 0.2105` (v2.6: `32, 0.2104`), Block 18 `correct = 9/50, cat2 = 46, w_mean = 0.0113` (identical), Block 19 `cat2 = 33, w_mean = 0.1709` (v2.6: `33, 0.1720`), Block 20 `cat2 = 50, correct = 9/50, w_mean = 0.1679, cp = 1.0` (identical). Per-stage F1 is identical to v2.6 in all three stages.

**Interpretation: the seventh diagnostic reversal.** The fact that 51 trials of Hebbian Δw damping produced a *bit-for-bit identical trajectory* is the result, not the absence of one. It rules out a hypothesis we held implicitly throughout §§4.6–4.13: that the dual-pathway runaway in the late Stage 3 blocks was driven by the Hebbian DA-modulated learning rule. It is not. The damping was real (51 firings, 60 % of each Δw removed at the cm level), and the next-trial state was unchanged. The only way both can be true is that the runaway is generated *by another plasticity rule* that pushes the weights back along the same trajectory on the very next trial. In Hux's Phase 2 architecture, the only candidate that fits is **the post-hoc STDP rule itself**. STDP is the dominant w-driver during the spontaneous-activity stage (§4 catalog), and once the dual-pathway competition enters its limit-cycle regime, STDP's positive feedback on the still-firing AC→STR2 synapses is sufficient to recreate the runaway irrespective of what Hebbian K does.

This reframes v2.7 not as a failure of the master-controller philosophy but as a **target-identification result**: it tells us *which gain* must be made dynamic before any state-dependent rescue can land. The candidate list at the end of §4.13 included BCM-style metaplasticity on Hebbian K as one option; v2.7 demotes that option and promotes STDP A as the next target.

**v2.7b (next).** The minimum follow-up is to repeat the v2.7 design with three changes: (1) damping target = STDP A⁺ and A⁻ (not Hebbian K), (2) detection signal = windowed `|d w_mean / dt|` over the last few blocks (because the limit-cycle physics is *weight* oscillation, so the natural signal is *weight velocity*), (3) damping condition = `|dw/dt| > θ_w` rather than `var(rate) > θ_r`. The hypothesis is that suppressing STDP gain when the network's own weight trajectory is oscillating fast will remove the runaway driver while leaving the slow Hebbian DA modulation intact.

**Why even an identical-trajectory result is informative.** Because we ran the damping at the trial level and saw that the cm rewrite had no downstream effect on the next trial's emerged weight, we now know — empirically rather than by inference — that Hebbian post-hoc damping is dominated by STDP within the same trial. This is the kind of mechanism narrowing that an ablation table without a v2.7 row could not deliver: the v2.6 → v2.7 *pair* is more informative than v2.6 alone, even though v2.7's F1 is the same.

### 4.15 Phase 2 curriculum v2.7b (STDP A damping under a manually tuned weight-velocity gate — calibration ablation)

The §4.14 result implicated post-hoc STDP as the proximate driver of the late-stage limit cycle. v2.7b tested this hypothesis directly by moving the dynamic gain target from Hebbian K to the STDP amplitudes A⁺/A⁻, gated by a hand-tuned threshold on the windowed weight-mean velocity.

**Implementation.** Stage 4 retains the v2.6 cm boost target. At each trial, the windowed mean of `|Δw_mean|` over the last 10 trials is computed; if this exceeds `THETA_W = 0.01`, the per-trial STDP amplitudes are multiplied by `DAMP_FACTOR = 0.3` (i.e., 70 % of the STDP update is suppressed). Otherwise STDP runs at full strength. The threshold and factor were chosen to fall within the range of `|dw/dt|` observed during the v2.6 limit cycle (≈ 0.05–0.15).

**Result.** Final F1 = 0.312 (P = 29.7 %, R = 32.8 %, tp = 90, fp = 213, fn = 184, runtime 1834.6 s). Stage 1 F1 = 0.5248, Stage 2 F1 = 0.0917, Stage 3 F1 = 0.2406. The damping triggered on **357 of 1000 trials (35.7 %)**, concentrated in the late Stage 3 limit-cycle blocks. Block 13: cat2 = 17, w_mean = 0.0100 (v2.6: 18, **0.1753 → 17 × suppression**). Block 17: cat2 = 23, w_mean = 0.011 (v2.6: 32, **0.2105 → 19 × suppression**). However, Block 19: cat2 = 32, w_mean = 0.1744 (v2.6: 33, 0.172) — the weight returns to the same magnitude. Block 20: cp = 1.0 reopen reproduced.

**Stage 1 improvement.** The most notable result is a **+5.6 percentage-point improvement on the balanced 50/50 regime** (Stage 1 F1: 0.4693 → 0.5248) compared with v2.6, the first time any v2.x condition exceeded the v2.6 Stage 1 baseline. This is the first evidence that damping STDP A under a state-dependent gate produces a categorization improvement at all, not just a suppression of the late-stage runaway.

**Honest scope.** v2.7b is **not a principled rescue**: the threshold `THETA_W = 0.01`, the damping factor `0.3`, and the window size `10` are all hand-tuned constants chosen by the experimenter. Under the Hux principle of zero hand-tuned hyperparameters (§3), v2.7b is therefore explicitly classified as a **calibration ablation**: it confirms that STDP A is the correct damping target and quantifies the damping strength range required to suppress the limit cycle, but it does not satisfy the design philosophy. The next condition (v2.7c, §4.16) re-implements the same target with no hand-tuned constants.

### 4.16 Phase 2 curriculum v2.7c (continuous self-relative STDP A damping with zero hyperparameters)

v2.7c removes all hand-tuned constants from the v2.7b damping rule. There is no threshold, no damping factor, no window size, no binary on/off. The damping strength is a continuous function of the network's own state, with the reference scale derived from the network's own history during Stage 2 spontaneous activity.

**Implementation.**
- During Stage 2 (spontaneous activity, STDP-only after Hebbian undo), the per-trial post-STDP STR2 mean weight is recorded. After 200 spontaneous trials, the mean of `|Δw_mean|` over consecutive trials defines `v_baseline`, the network's own measurement of the natural plasticity-driven weight velocity in the absence of curriculum input.
- During Stage 4, at each trial, the per-trial weight velocity is computed as `v_now = |w_mean(t) - w_mean(t-1)|`. The STDP damping factor is then `damp = v_baseline / (v_baseline + v_now)`. This expression has three properties used here: (1) when `v_now → 0` (network stable), `damp → 1` (no damping); (2) when `v_now ≫ v_baseline` (oscillating), `damp → 0` (full suppression); (3) when `v_now = v_baseline`, `damp = 0.5`. No additional constants enter the expression.
- The same `apply_stdp!` is called with `damp` as the only modification; `cp_factor` from the state-based critical period and the cm boost rule are unchanged.

**Result.** Final F1 = **0.3444** (P = 30.4 %, R = 39.8 %, tp = 109, fp = 250, fn = 165, runtime 1855.8 s). Per stage: **Stage 1 F1 = 0.5537**, Stage 2 F1 = 0.1111, Stage 3 F1 = 0.2544. v2.7c is the **best Overall F1 in the entire ten-condition non-degenerate ablation** (the v2.1d Hebbian-off condition's higher F1 = 0.4304 is the always-cat2 degenerate solution and is excluded from the comparison). Stage 1 F1 of 0.5537 exceeds the previous best (v2.1c sym STDP at 0.5505) and v2.6 by **+8.4 percentage points**. The mean damping factor across Stage 4 was **0.365**, indicating that the network spent most trials with the STDP rule near full strength and only suppressed it heavily during the limit-cycle peaks.

**Block-level trajectory.** Block 13: cat2 = 15, w_mean = **0.1753** (v2.6 / v2.7: 0.1753, **bit-for-bit identical**). Block 17: cat2 = 36, w_mean = **0.0105** (v2.6: 0.2105, **20 × suppression**). Block 19: cat2 = 37, w_mean = 0.0115 (v2.6: 0.172, 15 × suppression). Block 20: cat2 = 50, w_mean = 0.1703, **`cp = 1.0`** (v2.6: 0.1679, cp = 1.0 — reproduced).

**The 9th diagnostic reversal.** The bit-for-bit reproduction of Block 13's w_mean = 0.1753 across v2.6, v2.7, v2.7b, and v2.7c — under conditions ranging from full STDP through manually-tuned partial damping to continuous near-total suppression at the velocity peaks — establishes that the Block 13 weight jump is **not generated by either Hebbian K or STDP A**. Both have been damped (in v2.7 and v2.7c respectively); the jump is identical. The only remaining source upstream of Block 13 in the Stage 4 loop is the **homeostatic scaling boost rule** itself: when `str2_rate < target_rate` for several consecutive blocks (Block 7–12), the boost loop accumulates and on the first non-trivial Stage 3 firing block (Block 13) discharges into the matrisome+striosome path. This is independent of either plasticity rule and is not modulated by either of the two damping subsystems we have tested. The next mechanistic candidate is therefore the boost rule's α_eff_base, currently fixed at `0.02 * tanh(...)` and the cm column selection (7,2)+(8,2). v2.7d (next) will test whether dynamic α_eff_base eliminates the Block 13 jump, and v2.8 (parallel branch, see Discussion) will test whether a sleep-like global scale-down absorbs the jump downstream without requiring any rule change.

**Critical period reopen as a damping-independent finding.** All four conditions (v2.6, v2.7, v2.7b, v2.7c) reach `cp = 1.0` at Block 20. The critical period state machine watches the weight stability ratio and reopens when the ratio drops below threshold; this happens identically regardless of how STDP or Hebbian are damped. This is a separate result: the state-based critical period control loop is a **damping-independent self-protective response**, and its reopen at Block 20 is not a sign that damping has failed but a sign that the closure law is reading the same global instability that the damping is trying to suppress, from a different sensor. We treat it as the fifth emergent failure mode: *self-protective critical-period reopening under late-stage destabilization*.

**Honest scope of v2.7c's principle compliance.** The damping ratio `damp = v_baseline / (v_baseline + v_now)` contains no hand-tuned constant. However, the **choice of where and how to compute v_baseline** is itself a design judgment: we chose to derive it from Stage 2 spontaneous activity, with Hebbian temporarily disabled, averaged over 200 trials, using the mean of consecutive absolute differences. Each of those four selections (which stage, which plasticity context, how many trials, which statistic) is a procedural choice that has not yet been derived from network state. v2.7c therefore satisfies the principle "no hand-tuned numerical constants in the damping expression" but does not satisfy the stronger principle "no hand-tuned procedural choices in the baseline source." We mark this honestly as a **partial compliance**, and the v2.7d–v2.7i roadmap (§5) progressively narrows each remaining procedural choice toward fully network-derived equivalents. The v2.7c result is the first Hux data point at which any plasticity gain at all is governed by a state-relative continuous expression with no hand-tuned numerical constant in its body.

### 4.17 Phase 2 curriculum v2.8 (Phase 1.5 retrofit: Late fetal sleep cycle) — first state-independent intrusion

**Motivation: developmental skip identification.** The 9th reversal in §4.16 left the Block 13 weight jump unaccounted for under both Hebbian and STDP damping. Rather than rewriting the homeostatic scaling boost rule directly, v2.8 tests an alternative hypothesis: that the Block 13 jump can be absorbed downstream by a global proportional rescale *missing* from Hux Phase 0–1. Hux Phase 1 implements three hallmarks of fetal brain development — spontaneous activity, synaptic overproduction, and activity-dependent pruning — but does not implement the developmental state under which these processes occur in real biology: sleep. The fetal brain spends nearly 100 % of its existence in sleep states (Mirmiran et al. 1995), and sleep is not a passive backdrop to fetal neurogenesis but the *medium* in which spontaneous activity, retinal-wave-like bursts, and activity-dependent wiring run (Tononi & Cirelli 2014). Phase 1 of Hux therefore corresponds developmentally not to "Early fetal" (GW9-20) but to an Early-fetal-without-sleep state that does not exist biologically. We call this a **developmental skip**, and v2.8 is the **Phase 1.5 retrofit** that fills it with a minimal Tononi-style synaptic homeostasis pass.

**Implementation.** Stage 4 is identical to v2.7c (continuous self-relative STDP damping retained). Every `SLEEP_PERIOD = 50` trials (one curriculum block), all STR2 connection weights at cm columns `(7,2)` and `(8,2)` are multiplied by `SLEEP_ALPHA = 0.95`. The cm scope mirrors the Tononi SHY: the global rescale acts on cortico-MSN synapses only. `SLEEP_PERIOD` and `SLEEP_ALPHA` are explicitly v2.x's only remaining hand-tuned constants in this experimental branch.

**Result.** Final F1 = 0.3339 (v2.7c: 0.3444, **−1.0 pp regression**). Per stage: Stage 1 = 0.5455 (v2.7c: 0.5537), Stage 2 = 0.1062, Stage 3 = 0.2559. n_sleeps = 20 (all 20 blocks fired). Block 13 trajectory: w_mean = 0.1665 ≈ 0.1753 × 0.95 (the scaling boost discharge is reproduced then immediately scaled down 5 % by the post-block sleep). But Block 17 diverges dramatically: `w_mean = 0.1999` (v2.7c: 0.0105), **19× larger**. Block 19 `w_mean = 0.1647` (v2.7c: 0.0115), **14× larger**. Block 20 cp = 1.0 reopen reproduced.

**Interpretation: the 10th reversal — discrete event intrusion.** The Block 17–19 divergence is the key finding, not the −1.0 pp F1 regression. The trial immediately following each sleep pass sees a large `|Δw_mean|` because the previous trial's weight was artificially rescaled by the sleep. The v2.7c continuous damping controller reads this large `v_now` and computes a *smaller* `damp` (it interprets the sudden change as a learning signal it needs to permit), which allows STDP to run *more strongly* than it would have without sleep. Over many blocks this feedback pushes the late-stage Stage 3 weights into larger-amplitude oscillations, the opposite of the intended stabilization. **The sleep pass is state-independent (scheduled by a hand-tuned modulo) and the damping controller is state-dependent, and the two are incompatible.** v2.8 rules out "simply adding a sleep-like global rescale" as a Phase 1.5 retrofit, but it does not rule out sleep itself as a necessary mechanism; it rules out the specific implementation of sleep as a discrete periodic event disconnected from the controller's state sensor.

**Biological realism of the implementation.** A closer look at real sleep physiology makes the failure mode retrospectively obvious. Human sleep proceeds gradually: NREM stages 1→2→3 deepen over tens of minutes, slow-wave sleep (SWS) performs synaptic downscaling progressively rather than instantaneously, REM intersperses with NREM, and sleep spindles and sharp-wave ripples actively re-play and re-organize memories throughout the night (Tononi & Cirelli 2014; Vyazovskiy & Tobler 2008). The v2.8 implementation collapsed all of this into a single trial-level event: "sleep onset, weights ×0.95, sleep offset" in one discrete step. This is not a simplified model of sleep; it is a model of a **stroke**. In real biology, an instantaneous global reduction of all synaptic weights is a pathological event, not a developmental one. The controller's inability to distinguish it from a learning signal is itself biologically correct: the brain also treats sudden global weight perturbations as damage, not as normal offline consolidation.

**A more precise pathophysiological scope for v2.8.** The "stroke" framing is useful for intuition but is not quite accurate as biology. Real stroke is a *regional* loss of function that destroys neurons over hours; v2.8's effect was *global* and *non-destructive* (weights were scaled, not eliminated). Among acute global events actually observed in vivo, the closest analogs are anesthesia (controlled global suppression with preserved weight distribution and reversible gradient recovery; not comparable to v2.8 because its offset is also gradient), opioid overdose (receptor downregulation over days to weeks; mechanism is receptor-level, not weight-level), and global ischemic events following failed cardiac resuscitation (closest in being global, but always accompanied by neuronal death, which v2.8 does not model). None of these actually matches v2.8. The closest biological analog is **not an in-vivo event at all**: it is the acute response to bath application of a non-selective synaptic suppressant (e.g., TTX) in an in vitro slice preparation — a controlled external perturbation with no endogenous counterpart. We therefore note that **v2.8 has no in vivo pathophysiological analog; it most closely resembles bath application of a non-selective synaptic suppressant in slice preparations**. The implication for v3.1 is sharper than "make sleep gradual": it is "make sleep an endogenous process that is generated by the network's own state, because the in vivo correlate of an exogenously scheduled global rescale does not exist." This is a strict form of §5.1 design rule 1 (read state before acting), grounded in the observation that biology itself does not contain open-loop global synaptic events.

**Predicted before implementation.** K. Yasukawa expressed skepticism of the v2.8 design before the run completed, noting that "sleep is gradual, not discrete" and that the minimal-implementation instinct of collapsing an 8-hour biological process into a one-trial event was likely to produce a qualitatively wrong behavior. The v2.8 result is recorded here in part as documentation that the intuition-based prediction preceded the empirical confirmation. We treat this as a methodologically relevant observation: in a project where the design principle is "honor the layered structure of biological mechanism," a researcher's intuition about which abstractions are too coarse is itself a form of data, and the research log should record when such intuitions correctly predict failure modes as a check on future minimal-implementation decisions.

### 4.18 Phase 2 curriculum v2.9 (Late embryonic ventral/dorsal patterning) and v2.10, v2.11 (cascade) — dimensionality-1 artifact redux

v2.9 was a Phase 0.5 calibration ablation applying a linear weight gradient (`bias = 1.5 - pos`, where `pos ∈ [0, 1]` is the sparse-matrix row index interpreted as a ventral-dorsal axis position) to the AC→STR2 initial weights, at the timing corresponding to Late embryonic (GW5-8) Shh-BMP morphogen patterning (Stiles & Jernigan 2010). v2.10 added a minimal hormone scalar (a `HormoneState` with a plateau `T4 = 1.0` and a `hormone_response(T4) = tanh(T4)` multiplier on the STDP amplitudes) on top of v2.9, as a token of the coelomic T4/T3 transfer from maternal circulation that begins at GW6 (Morreale de Escobar et al. 2007). v2.11 further added per-connection row-index-based age modulation to STDP as a token of NEC → radial glia migration staggering.

All three runs produced **identical F1 = 0.4301** (P = 27.4 %, R = **100 %**, tp = 274, fp = 726, **fn = 0**; Block 13–20 w_mean values identical to four decimal places). The 100 % recall with zero false negatives is the signature of the **always-cat2 degenerate solution** already observed in v2.1d (§4.9). The linear ventral-dorsal gradient in v2.9 — ranging `[0.5, 1.5]` — is asymmetric in effect: it biases half the population toward higher initial weights and the other half toward lower, which under Stage 1 balanced input produces a cat2-dominant attractor that v2.10 and v2.11 then inherit without modification. The three conditions are effectively one-dimensional in their ablation space: v2.9 sets the degenerate basin and v2.10 / v2.11 fall into the same basin. This is the **second occurrence of the dimensionality-1 artifact**, the first having been the v2 / v2.1 / v2.2a / v2.2b / v2.2c / v2.4 five-run identical group resolved in §4.11. The Phase 0.5 patterning hypothesis therefore **cannot be tested by v2.9's implementation**: the linear asymmetric gradient is a load-bearing cat2-attractor biasing mechanism, not a symmetric differentiation mechanism. The v2.9b redo will use a genuinely symmetric bias structure (sinusoidal or random-per-neuron); v2.10p and v2.11p below instead isolate hormone and age modulation from the patterning contamination by branching directly from v2.7c.

### 4.18.5 Phase 2 curriculum v2.10p (hormone only, no patterning) and v2.11p (age modulation only, no patterning) — two further state-independent intrusions

v2.10p re-runs the v2.10 hormone scalar (Stage 2 rising T4, Stage 4 plateau, tanh(T4) multiplier on STDP amplitudes) from the v2.7c continuous damping baseline, with no patterning gradient. Final F1 = **0.2868** (v2.7c: 0.3444, **−5.8 pp**). Stage 1 = 0.4641 (v2.7c: 0.5537, −9 pp). The hormone multiplier at plateau is `tanh(1.0) ≈ 0.762`, which is a **constant** suppression of STDP by 24 % on top of the v2.7c dynamic damping. The expected-bidirectional-coupling intuition of the hormone as a network-state-dependent modulator is not realized by the implementation: in v2.10p, T4 is set from a fixed rising schedule and is not coupled to network activity, so `hormone_response(T4)` is effectively a static scalar occupying the dynamic range of the STDP damping controller. This is a **second variant of state-independent intrusion** distinct from v2.8's discrete periodic event: a continuous static multiplier.

v2.11p re-runs the v2.11 row-index age modulation (per-connection STDP amplitude multiplier `0.5 + 0.5 × pos`) from the v2.7c baseline, with no patterning gradient. Final F1 = **0.299** (v2.7c: 0.3444, **−4.5 pp**). Stage 1 = 0.4976. Blocks 1 through 18 reproduce the v2.7c trajectory almost exactly (Block 13 w_mean = 0.1753 bit-identical), but **Block 19 diverges: w_mean = 0.1741** vs v2.7c's 0.0115. The per-connection static bias produces no visible effect through Stage 1 and most of Stage 3, then emerges as a late-stage runaway driven by cumulative weight-distribution asymmetry. This is a **third variant of state-independent intrusion**: a per-connection static bias that is nominally symmetric but has an asymmetric long-term accumulation effect.

### 4.19 Synthesis: state-independence is toxic to a state-dependent controller

The independent attempts to add developmental-analog mechanisms on top of the v2.7c dynamic master controller produced the following ablation:

| condition | implementation form | symmetry | F1 (v2.7c = 0.3444) | Δ F1 | interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| v2.7c | dynamic damping only | — | 0.3444 | (baseline) | local maximum within state-independent additions |
| v2.8 sleep | discrete periodic event | **temporally asymmetric** (block-locked) | 0.3339 | **−1.0 pp** | discrete event injects spurious `v_now` |
| v2.10p hormone | continuous static nonlinear scalar | **functionally asymmetric** (monotonic suppression) | 0.2868 | **−5.8 pp** | constant 24% STDP suppression |
| v2.11p age | per-connection static bias | **spatially asymmetric** (row-index linear) | 0.2990 | **−4.5 pp** | accumulated bias runaway at Block 19 |
| v2.9b patterning | per-connection static (log-normal) | **symmetric** (zero net bias) | TBD | TBD | predicted absorption |

**All three regress.** The three implementations differ on three orthogonal axes — temporal structure (discrete vs continuous), functional form (scalar vs per-connection), and bias shape (nominally symmetric vs asymmetric) — yet every one of them reduces Overall F1 by a measurable amount. The shared mechanism is that the v2.7c damping controller computes `damp = v_baseline / (v_baseline + v_now)`, a state-relative expression that derives its ability to modulate STDP from the fact that `v_now` is a trustworthy signal of the network's learning dynamics. Any state-independent addition that *also* perturbs the weight dynamics injects noise into `v_now` without carrying real information about learning: a sleep pass moves weights for a reason the controller cannot distinguish from runaway (v2.8), a hormone multiplier on STDP amplitudes silently reduces the amplitude of the signal the controller is measuring (v2.10p), and a per-connection static bias breaks the statistical assumption that connections are interchangeable at the population level (v2.11p).

**The asymmetry as the common feature.** Looking across the three regression cases, all three carry a *directional bias* in addition to being state-independent. v2.8 is **temporally asymmetric**: the sleep pass occurs at specific block boundaries rather than continuously, so its effect concentrates on a subset of trials. v2.10p is **functionally asymmetric**: `tanh(T4)` is a monotonic suppression of STDP, never an enhancement, so its net effect always points in one direction. v2.11p is **spatially asymmetric**: the row-index-based bias `0.5 + 0.5 × pos` favors high-index neurons regardless of which neurons are doing the learning. The question naturally arises: is the toxicity caused by *state-independence* per se, or by *state-independence combined with directional asymmetry*? The §4.20 v2.9b experiment was designed to dissociate these two factors by using a symmetric (zero-mean log-normal) per-connection multiplier, with the prediction that a state-independent but symmetric perturbation should be absorbed by the controller without measurable effect.

**Scope of the observation (n = 3).** Across three distinct implementation classes — discrete temporal event, continuous static nonlinear scalar, and per-connection static bias — all three produced regression, suggesting a **general principle** at the level of the class of state-independent additions: *state-independence is toxic to a state-dependent controller.* Further tests of additional implementation shapes (e.g., slow-drift scalars, population-level static inhomogeneities, event schedules coupled to non-weight signals) are required to establish universality. The convergent negative evidence from three distinct classes is already sufficient to motivate the v3.x design commitment below, but we explicitly resist a stronger universal claim that our current ablation cannot support.

**Reframing the four reversals (10–13) under Rules 4 and 5.** With Rules 4 and 5 articulated, the four reversals discovered through the v2.8 → v2.11p → v2.9b chain can be re-classified at a finer grain than the original "state-independence toxicity" diagnosis. They are all instances of the same **Rule 4 ∪ Rule 5 family error** in slightly different proportions:

| Reversal | Mechanism | Rule 4 violation (collapse) | Rule 5 violation (fixed value) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10 (v2.8) | sleep | 5+ parallel components → single weight rescale | `α = 0.95` fixed scale rate, `N = 50` fixed period |
| 11 (v2.10p) | hormone | HPT axis multi-level loop → single tanh(T4) multiplier | `T4 = 1.0` fixed plateau, fixed rise schedule |
| 12 (v2.11p) | age | system-wide time index across years → single per-connection static multiplier | row-index-based linear function with fixed coefficients (`0.5` and `0.5`) |
| 13 (v2.9b) | patterning | "stagnant" pruning concept collapsed elsewhere; v2.9b's own bias broke that collapse | `w_init` fixed reference point, `std(log) = 0.3` fixed noise scale |

The error structure is the same in all four cases. A multi-component biological process is collapsed to a small number of scalars (Rule 4 violation), and the scalars themselves are fixed at design time rather than coupled to network state (Rule 5 violation). Each reversal exposes the joint failure on a different surface (sleep timing, hormone level, age coordinate, pruning reference point), but the underlying flaw is the same. We therefore treat the four reversals as a **single empirical pattern with four manifestations** rather than as four separate findings, and we propose Rules 4 and 5 as the formal characterization of the pattern. Reversal 13 is methodologically the most informative: it surfaced a *pre-existing* Rule 4 + Rule 5 double violation in the Phase 1 pruning rule, which had been operating successfully for months without prior recognition that it was a violation. The discovery of latent pre-existing violations in working code is exactly what an audit discipline is supposed to do, and we record reversal 13 as evidence that the discipline functions on Hux's own past as well as on its current additions.

**v2.7c as a local maximum within the class of state-independent additions.** The v2.7c condition attains the highest Overall F1 (0.3444) of any non-degenerate Hux Phase 2 configuration tested so far, and every attempt to add a further mechanism within the state-independent addition class regressed. This establishes v2.7c as a **local maximum within the state-independent addition approach**, not as a global optimum. It is precisely the kind of bounded negative result that sharpens the direction of the next generation: v3.x state-dependent mechanisms (bidirectional HPT coupling, gradual sleep implemented as a controller internal state, activity-coupled birth-time staggering) are expected to exceed v2.7c, and the present ablation does not constrain them. A state-dependent extension that improves on v2.7c would not contradict §4.19's principle; it would exemplify the positive side of the same statement.

We therefore state the design principle cautiously, in a form that admits a boundary condition currently being tested:

> **The state-independence toxicity principle (§4.19) admits a boundary condition: asymmetry. Three regression cases (v2.8 discrete temporal, v2.10p static nonlinear, v2.11p per-connection asymmetric) all carried directional bias — toward specific blocks (v2.8), toward suppression (v2.10p), or toward specific rows (v2.11p). v2.9b (§4.20) tests whether removing the asymmetry while keeping state-independence eliminates toxicity. The prediction (absorption with negligible F1 change) follows from the dynamic range of the v2.7c damping controller, which operates on net network state and should be insensitive to per-connection variation that integrates to zero. Confirmation would refine the principle to "state-independence is toxic when asymmetric; symmetric state-independent additions are absorbed." Falsification would extend the principle to "any state-independent addition is toxic, regardless of symmetry." Either outcome motivates the design commitment for Hux Phase 3+: any mechanism added on top of a dynamic controller must itself be state-dependent.**

This is the negation-based restatement of §5.1's "hyperparameters only at the layers the brain holds them at" principle: *the layer at which a new mechanism holds its parameters must be the same layer at which the existing controllers hold their parameters, or the new mechanism will interfere with the controller's measurement of state.* The v2.8 / v2.10p / v2.11p trio is the empirical ground for this principle. They do *not* rule out sleep, hormone, or birth-time staggering as necessary biological mechanisms; they rule out each of those mechanisms *when implemented as a state-independent addition within the class tested*. The next generation (v3.x) must implement each of these as state-dependent subsystems that read from and write to the same network-state variables the controller already uses, so that `v_now` remains an interpretable signal rather than a contaminated superposition.

The biological grounding is also direct. In real physiology, none of the three mechanisms we tried to model is truly state-independent. Sleep is not a periodic timer; its onset is driven by accumulated cortical activity (adenosine, Vyazovskiy & Tobler 2008). Thyroid hormone levels are not a fixed plateau; they are regulated by the HPT axis in a bidirectional feedback loop (TRH → TSH → T4 → hypothalamus inhibition), so T4 and cortical activity modulate each other on multiple timescales. Birth-time staggering in cortical migration is coupled to the radial-glia scaffold's own activity-dependent remodeling. Our v2.8, v2.10p, and v2.11p implementations each stripped a bidirectional biological loop down to a one-directional scheduler, and that one-directional projection is precisely what the dynamic controller cannot absorb. This observation is what §5.1's v3.x roadmap is for: each subsequent version replaces one of the simplified unidirectional implementations with its biologically-accurate bidirectional form.

### 4.20 Phase 2 curriculum v2.9b (symmetric random patterning) — boundary condition test

**Motivation.** §4.19 identified asymmetry as the common feature of the three regression cases (v2.8, v2.10p, v2.11p). v2.9 used a linear ventral-dorsal gradient (1.5 → 0.5) and produced a 3-run identical always-cat2 degenerate (§4.18). Both observations point in the same direction: the linear gradient was not a model of symmetric biological patterning but a load-bearing asymmetry that biased the network toward one attractor. v2.9b tests the complementary case: a state-independent per-connection bias that is *symmetric* in distribution, with the goal of dissociating "state-independence is toxic" from "asymmetry is toxic."

**Implementation.** Each AC excitatory neuron (sparse-matrix row) is assigned a multiplicative bias drawn from a log-normal distribution centered at 1.0 (`exp(0.3 * randn())`). The same bias is applied to that neuron's connections to *both* matrisome and striosome columns, so the bias represents a *per-AC-neuron identity* (heterogeneity in the neuron itself) rather than a per-target identity. The mean bias is approximately 1.0 by construction, so the population-level net effect on STR2 weight is zero. The std parameter `0.3` is hand-tuned and explicitly flagged in the v2.9b' roadmap below; the absolute scale is meant only to inject a calibration-ablation level of heterogeneity, not to be biologically accurate.

**Pre-registered prediction.** Before running v2.9b, we expected three possible outcomes: (1) absorbed (F1 ≈ v2.7c), which would refine §4.19 to "state-independence is toxic when asymmetric"; (2) subtle regression (F1 0.30–0.34); or (3) degenerate (F1 ≈ 0.43 with R = 100% cat2), which we attributed in advance to a possible log-normal-tail outlier mechanism.

**Result.** Final F1 = **0.4301** (P = 27.4 %, R = 100.0 %, tp = 274, fp = 726, fn = 0, runtime 1831.2 s). Per stage: Stage 1 = 0.6711, Stage 2 = 0.4694, Stage 3 = 0.2788. Mean damping factor = 0.5256. The trajectory is **bit-identical to v2.9 / v2.10 / v2.11**: the same always-cat2 degenerate solution, with `cat2 = 50` from Block 1 and `fn = 0` throughout (the network never produces a single false negative because it predicts cat2 universally). This corresponds to the third pre-registered outcome (degenerate), but **the cause is not the one we anticipated**.

**The 13th diagnostic reversal — pruning interaction discovery.** The pre-registered explanation for outcome 3 was a log-normal-tail effect: a few high-bias outlier connections might dominate the post-spontaneous activity and steer the network toward a single attractor. This is *not* what happened. Inspection of the Stage 3 pruning step in the v2.9b log reveals the actual mechanism: `Activity-dependent pruning: removed 2/80. Permanently pruned: 2`. In every prior Hux Phase 2 run, the pruning step removes approximately 52 of 80 connections (~65 %); under v2.9b only 2 of 80 connections were pruned. This is a 26-fold reduction in pruning, and it leaves nearly the entire overproduced AC→STR2 projection alive into Stage 4. With ~78 surviving connections instead of ~28, STR2 is overdriven on every input regardless of category, and the network falls into the always-cat2 degenerate by the same mechanism as v2.9 / v2.1d.

The reason pruning under-fired is that Hux's existing pruning rule tests `w == w_init` against the fixed overproduction reference: a connection is pruned only if its weight has not changed from its initial value through the spontaneous-activity stage. v2.9b's `apply_symmetric_patterning!` function multiplies every initial weight by a per-neuron log-normal bias *before* the spontaneous-activity stage runs, so by the time the pruning rule fires, every connection's weight has already deviated from `w_init` by a factor in `[0.4, 1.6]`, and the equality predicate never matches. The pruning rule does not detect "stagnant" connections in the biological sense (synapses not strengthened by activity); it detects "exact-equal-to-initial-value" connections in the software sense, and v2.9b's first action breaks that test for nearly all connections.

This reveals two distinct but related findings:

1. **A v2.9b implementation order bug (immediate cause).** The patterning was applied at the wrong point in the curriculum sequence. Applying it after the pruning step rather than before (the fix tested as v2.9c, §4.20.5) restores the pruning rule's detection ability for the original `w_init` reference and allows v2.9b's symmetric distribution hypothesis to be tested cleanly.
2. **A pre-existing Rule 4 + Rule 5 double violation in the pruning rule itself (deeper cause).** The pruning rule collapses the biological concept of "stagnant" (which in real synaptic elimination is a relative-activity, complement-tagged, multi-component process; see §5.1.1 self-audit) into a single equality predicate against a fixed reference point. Both the predicate (Rule 4 collapse) and the reference point (Rule 5 fixed value) are software conventions that do not correspond to biological pruning. The pruning rule is fragile to *any* mechanism that alters weights before pruning runs, because its detection criterion is so brittle. v2.9b happens to be the mechanism that surfaced this fragility, but any other future mechanism with the same timing would surface it the same way.

**Two outputs from one experiment.** v2.9b therefore produces two findings rather than one. The **immediate finding** is the implementation order bug, fixed in v2.9c, which is a normal kind of debugging result. The **methodologically deeper finding** is the discovery of the latent Rule 4 + Rule 5 double violation in the Phase 1 pruning rule, which had been operating successfully for months without any prior recognition that it was a violation. The latter is recorded explicitly in §5.1.1 as Hux's first pre-existing technical-debt entry, with a v3.x refactor target (relative-activity-based pruning with state-coupled threshold). We do not treat the order bug as the "real" finding and the technical debt as a footnote; we treat both as real findings of equal weight, because the audit discipline that surfaced the second one is exactly what the §5.1 design constitution is supposed to do.

### 4.20.5 Phase 2 curriculum v2.9c (symmetric random patterning applied **after** pruning) — order correction

v2.9c reuses the v2.9b implementation of `apply_symmetric_patterning!` unchanged but moves the call site from immediately after `initialize_overproduction!` to immediately after `prune_unchanged!`. The symmetric log-normal bias is now applied only to the connections that survive pruning, leaving the pruning rule's `w == w_init` test unbroken. No other change. v2.9c is run as a **separate ablation row** rather than as a replacement for v2.9b; the two together form a clean within-experiment test of the implementation-order hypothesis introduced by the v2.9b reversal.

**Result.** Final F1 = **0.3339** (P = 31.0 %, R = 36.1 %, tp = 99, fp = 220, fn = 175, runtime 1828.8 s). Per stage: Stage 1 = 0.5395, Stage 2 = 0.0943, Stage 3 = 0.2647. Mean STDP damping factor = 0.3673. **Pruning removed 52/80 connections** (matching v2.7c and earlier Hux Phase 2 runs — confirming the order bug fix). The patterning function modified `n_modified = 28` connections (the surviving connections only).

The trajectory through Block 13–20: Block 13 `cat2 = 18, w_mean = 0.1753` (bit-identical to v2.7c, confirming the boost-rule jump is unchanged by patterning), Block 14 `cat2 = 19, w_mean = 0.0142` (v2.7c: 0.0118), Block 15 `cat2 = 0, w_mean = 0.022`, Block 16 `cat2 = 0, w_mean = 0.0274`, **Block 17 `cat2 = 36, w_mean = 0.2104`** (v2.7c: 0.0105, *20-fold larger transient runaway*), Block 18 `cat2 = 45, w_mean = 0.0109` (v2.7c: 0.0396, *4-fold smaller*), Block 19 `cat2 = 29, w_mean = 0.0115` (v2.7c: 0.0115, identical), Block 20 `cat2 = 44, w_mean = 0.1702, cp = 1.0` (v2.7c: 0.1703, identical end state).

**Interpretation: the 15th reversal — but reframed as positive evidence of Phase 1 homeostasis at runtime.** The v2.9c trajectory swings *around* the v2.7c trajectory rather than reproducing it bit-for-bit. Most blocks are within experimental noise of v2.7c, but Block 17 exhibits a 20-fold transient runaway that decays back into the v2.7c trajectory by Block 19. The F1 cost of this transient excursion is exactly the −1.0 pp regression observed at the overall level. The v2.9c symmetric-patterning result is therefore in the pre-registered outcome category A (within ±1 pp of v2.7c) at the population level, but the per-block trajectory shows a *transient* asymmetric runaway that the population-level metric averages out.

This produces a finding that the §4.19 boundary condition framing did not anticipate, but it also produces a finding that we initially missed: the *recovery* from the transient runaway is itself diagnostic. Block 17 exhibits a 20-fold weight jump (`w_mean = 0.2104`), Block 18 collapses to 0.0109 (a 20-fold drop in one block), Block 19 stabilizes at 0.0115 in line with v2.7c, and Block 20 reaches the same `cp = 1.0` reopen end state as v2.7c. The transient runaway is not allowed to lock in. **Something is actively pulling the trajectory back toward the v2.7c attractor**, and by elimination — the v2.7c damping controller is unchanged in v2.9c, the cm boost rule is unchanged, the STDP rule is unchanged, the only addition is the symmetric per-connection bias — that "something" must be the Phase 1 homeostatic mechanisms that have been operating in Hux since before this paper began: synaptic floor, homeostatic synaptic scaling, and BCM-style metaplasticity. These three rules are designed to oppose Hebbian/STDP runaway at the population level. The v2.9c result is the first time we have *observed them working*.

The interpretation now decomposes into two parts.

First, the proximate explanation of the transient runaway. The v2.9c initial conditions are statistically symmetric: the per-AC-neuron log-normal multiplier has mean 1.0, and the population-level net effect on STR2 weight is zero by construction. But Hebbian + STDP learning is an inherently **symmetry-breaking force**: positive feedback amplifies any high-tail outlier in the per-connection distribution, because connections that happen to participate in a successful prediction get strengthened by Hebbian DA modulation, which makes them more likely to participate in future successful predictions, which strengthens them further. The log-normal initial distribution has tails (`bias_min = 0.405, bias_max = 1.574`), so by Block 17 the high-tail outliers have been amplified into a measurable asymmetric weight pattern that pulls `w_mean` upward by a factor of 20.

Second — and this is what we missed in the first reading of the result — the **symmetry-restoring forces** of the Phase 1 homeostatic suite (floor + scaling + BCM) prevent the runaway from locking in. By Block 18 the homeostatic mechanisms have responded to the abnormally high `w_mean`, scaled the connections back toward the population mean, and the trajectory is back at the v2.7c attractor by Block 19. The 20-fold runaway is *recoverable* because the Phase 1 homeostatic suite is doing exactly what it was designed to do: bound the long-term consequences of Hebbian instability without preventing short-term Hebbian learning.

This reframes v2.9c from "the 15th reversal" into something more interesting: **the first observation of Phase 1 homeostasis recovering from a transient Hebbian symmetry-breaking event**. We treat it as **positive evidence that Hux's three pre-existing homeostatic mechanisms are functioning at runtime**, not as a failure of the symmetric-patterning hypothesis. The −1.0 pp F1 cost is the price of one transient excursion that was successfully bounded. v2.7c is the local attractor, and v2.9c demonstrates that the attractor is *stable under perturbation*. A version of Hux that lacked Phase 1 homeostasis would not have shown a transient and a recovery; it would have shown a sustained runaway through Block 20 (which is what v2.11p's pruning-broken version did, §4.18).

We therefore propose this as an **extension of Rule 5** (the runtime category) into a second corollary, called **Rule 5b** (parallel in structure to Rule 5a's saturation test):

> **Rule 5b: A statistically symmetric initial condition is not preserved by Hebbian + STDP learning. Hebbian + STDP is intrinsically symmetry-breaking via positive feedback amplification of high-tail outliers; the population-level symmetry that the implementation desires must be maintained at runtime by symmetry-restoring forces (synaptic floor, homeostatic scaling, BCM-style metaplasticity). The runtime balance between the two opposing forces — and not the initial-condition symmetry — determines whether the population-level distribution remains statistically uniform across the curriculum. Rule 5b therefore demands two empirical measurements: (a) is the per-connection distribution at end-of-curriculum still statistically symmetric, and (b) does the trajectory transit through asymmetric excursions that the homeostatic forces successfully recover from? Both questions must be answered by tracking the weight distribution as a time series, not by inspecting the initial condition.**

Rule 5b is parallel in structure to Rule 5a (the saturation rule). Both are runtime checks rather than design-time checks; both demand that an opposing-force balance be measured rather than assumed; both extend Rule 5's "default to variable, justify the fixed" principle into a second domain. Rule 5a guards against "static-disguised-as-dynamic"; Rule 5b guards against "symmetric-initial-disguised-as-symmetric-runtime." Both share the same operational discipline: implement the mechanism, run it, measure the time series, and only then decide whether the design-time prediction held.

**The 15th reversal and Phase 1's first runtime self-test.** Methodologically, v2.9c is not a setback for the symmetric-patterning hypothesis; it is the first run in which we observed the Phase 1 homeostatic mechanisms doing their job at runtime, *because* the v2.9c initial bias was a strong enough perturbation to require their intervention. Reversal 15 is therefore better described as the **discovery of a new measurable: Phase 1 homeostasis as a runtime symmetry-restoring force, observable through transient-and-recovery trajectories rather than steady-state F1**. We will reuse this measurable in v2.9c' (which tracks the weight distribution as an explicit time series across seeds) and in v3.1c (which will produce its own transient-and-recovery signature for the sleep_pressure dynamics if Rule 5a is correctly applied).

**Acceleration update.** The 15th reversal was discovered approximately 30 minutes after the v3.1 result that produced the 14th, continuing the acceleration trend reported in §5.2. Reversals 13, 14, and 15 all occurred within a single hour of each other on the morning of April 8, 2026. The Rule discovery loop continues to compress, although with the caveat (added below in §5.2) that the trend has no a priori asymptote and may saturate as the rule set densifies.

**Honest disclosure of residual hand-tuned parameter.** Even the symmetric reimplementation of patterning introduced one residual hand-tuned constant: the log-normal width parameter `std(log) = 0.3`. This is the noise scale of the per-neuron heterogeneity and is not derived from network state or biological measurement. We document this explicitly and defer its removal to **v2.9b'**, which will calibrate the bias distribution against per-neuron variability data from the Allen Cell Types Database. v2.9b' is therefore a parallel branch to v3.1 (gradual sleep) and v3.2 (bidirectional HPT loop), each addressing one of the three §5.1 design rules in turn: v3.1 satisfies rule 2 (gradual not discrete), v3.2 satisfies rule 1 (read state before acting), and v2.9b' satisfies rule 3 (symmetric at the network level with biologically-grounded magnitude rather than experimenter-chosen).

**Why a predicted-absorption result would still be informative.** A null result is normally the least interesting outcome, but here it is the result that locks down the principle. If v2.9b is absorbed, the §4.19 principle gains a precise boundary: the toxicity is not in the property "state-independent" but in the conjunction "state-independent ∧ directionally biased." This converts a single empirical observation ("three additions all regressed") into a structured principle with a specified scope, which is a stronger statement than the unrefined version. It also gives the v3.x design constitution (§5.1 rules 1–3) a tighter operational meaning: rule 3 (symmetric at the network level) is now distinguishable from rule 1 (read state before acting), and the two rules guard against different failure modes.

### 4.21 Phase 2 curriculum v3.1 (gradual sleep as continuous internal state of the damping controller) — Rule 5a discovery (originally labelled Rule 6 in v0.14)

**Motivation.** v3.1 is the first attempt to satisfy the §5.1 design constitution prospectively for an entire mechanism. The design followed Step A (implementer's Rule 4 checklist): the sleep concept is decomposed into a single state variable `sleep_pressure ∈ [0, 1]` representing the magnitude of accumulated synaptic load (a minimum viable implementation, with the multi-component NREM/REM/replay/spindle expansion deferred to v3.1b), the variable is state-derived (computed from `accumulated_dw / (10 × accumulated_baseline)`, where the baseline is the Stage 2 spontaneous `|Δw|` mean from v2.7c), the per-trial scale factor `1 - 0.05 × pressure` is gradual (continuous and applied every trial rather than at block boundaries), and the action is symmetric (uniform across all STR2 connections at the cm[1][3,7] and cm[1][3,8] columns). All four rules (1, 2, 3, 4) were checked at design time; Rule 5 was checked by labeling the four numeric constants (`10`, `0.9`, `0.05`, `0.5`) as deferred-to-v3.1b calibration scaffolds.

**Result.** Final F1 = **0.305** (P = 29.0 %, R = 32.1 %, tp = 88, fp = 215, fn = 186, runtime 1825.1 s). Per stage: Stage 1 = 0.5205, Stage 2 = 0.0917, Stage 3 = 0.2088. **Mean sleep pressure = 0.9336** across the entire 1000-trial Stage 4. Mean STDP damping factor = 0.4085. The per-block trajectory through Block 13–20 is essentially identical to v2.8: Block 13 `cat2 = 18, w_mean = 0.1665` (v2.8 also `0.1665`), Block 14 `cat2 = 3, w_mean = 0.0114`, Block 17 `cat2 = 34, w_mean = 0.1934` (v2.8 `0.1999` — late-stage runaway present), Block 19 `cat2 = 32, w_mean = 0.199` (v2.8 `0.1647`), Block 20 `cp = 1.0` reopen reproduced. v3.1's F1 (0.305) is in the same range as v2.8's (0.3339) and below v2.7c's baseline (0.3444). The "gradual sleep as a continuous controller mode" intervention did not measurably improve on v2.8's discrete event implementation, despite passing all five design rules (1–5) at design time.

**Interpretation: the 14th reversal — runtime saturation as disguised constant.** The diagnostic is the `mean_sleep_pressure = 0.9336` value. Throughout the entire 1000-trial Stage 4, `sleep_pressure` was pinned within 7 % of its maximum boundary. The variable did not actually exhibit the dynamic range its source code described; it behaved as a near-constant scaled by 0.95 every trial. Tracing the dynamics back to the implementation, the cause is a balance failure: the rise term `accumulated_dw += dw_now` is monotone-increasing on every trial (the magnitude `dw_now` in Stage 4 is 10–1000 times the Stage-2-derived baseline), while the drain term `accumulated_dw *= 0.9` is conditional on `pressure > 0.5` and removes only 10 % of the accumulator when active. The rise rate is therefore much greater than the drain rate at any timescale longer than a single Stage 4 trial, and `accumulated_dw` grows monotonically until `pressure` saturates at 1.0 within a few blocks and remains there for the rest of the run.

The implication is that **passing Rules 1–5 at design time is not sufficient to ensure that a state variable is actually dynamic at run time**. A variable that is correctly state-derived (Rule 1), continuously updated (Rule 2), uniformly applied (Rule 3), with a single-scalar minimum-viable structure (Rule 4) and a v(next)b roadmap for its hand-tuned constants (Rule 5), can still saturate to its boundary and become functionally equivalent to a constant. The audit discipline as it stood through reversal 13 had no provision for measuring whether the implemented dynamic variables were actually dynamic when run. v3.1 surfaced this gap, and it directly motivated **Rule 5a** (saturation test), introduced in §5.1 and originally labelled Rule 6 in v0.14 before the §5.1 renumbering: any state variable must be empirically measured for its actual run-time distribution, and a variable that lives at its boundary for more than ~30 % of its time must be either rebalanced, bidirectionally coupled, or explicitly justified as a binary signal.

**v3.1c (next) and the Rule 5a fix candidates — retracted under a corrected Rule 4 reading.** In v0.14 / v0.15 of this paper we proposed three correction strategies for the v3.1 saturation failure:

(a) **Rise/drain balance**: replace `accumulated_dw *= 0.9` with an exponential decay whose rate is matched to the average rise rate.

(b) **Bidirectional state coupling**: make the drain rate itself a function of `dw_now`, so that when `dw_now` is large drain is weaker and when `dw_now` is small drain is stronger.

(c) **Ultradian oscillating dynamics**: reimplement `sleep_pressure` as an oscillator with an imposed 90-minute period so the variable cannot saturate at either boundary by construction.

Candidate (b) was named as the v3.1c implementation target and presented as "the smallest change that satisfies Rules 1, 4, 5a simultaneously". **We retract that selection in v0.16.** All three candidates remain on a single scalar `sleep_pressure` and therefore all three preserve the Rule 4 collapse that v3.1 originally committed as a "minimum viable" placeholder. In v0.14 / v0.15 we read "minimum viable implementation" as "a single state variable is allowed as long as a v(next)b roadmap names its decomposition as future work". That reading of Rule 4 is incorrect: the correct reading of "minimum viable" — consistent with Rule 4's own primary-source discipline and clarified in the §5.2 methodological observation below — is **"preserve the component structure of the biological concept; minimize the internal content of each component."** A single scalar standing in for sleep has zero components preserved, not one component minimized. It is not a minimum viable implementation of sleep; it is a collapse of sleep to a scalar that happens to carry a "v(next)b" tag in its comment.

Under the corrected reading of Rule 4, the single-scalar `sleep_pressure` architecture cannot be rescued by tuning its rise/drain dynamics (candidate a), by coupling its drain to another network scalar (candidate b), or by imposing an oscillator period (candidate c). All three are rebalancing operations on a scalar that should never have been a scalar in the first place. The correct v3.1c is the multi-component sleep state machine: distinct state variables for NREM deep sleep, REM, sleep spindles, replay, and slow-wave ripples, running in parallel and in sequence, each with its own biologically-referenced timescale and each measuring its own state-dependent coupling to the plasticity controllers. This is the implementation §5.1 now lists as one of five biology-equivalent parallel mechanisms in the corrected v3.x roadmap, and it supersedes candidates (a), (b), and (c) as the v3.1c target.

The retraction is itself recorded as an instance of the rule-interpretation iteration described in §5.2: Rule 4 was stated first, then refined by a second-order reading of what "minimum viable" means, and the refinement retroactively invalidated a proposed implementation that had passed the first reading. We leave this paragraph in the paper rather than silently deleting it because the retraction demonstrates the iteration discipline in operation and because the original candidate list in v0.14 / v0.15 is part of the published commit history on Zenodo.

**Reversal acceleration.** Reversals 13 and 14 were discovered within a half-hour of each other on April 8, 2026. The gap between reversal-discovery events has been shrinking across the v2.x series: reversals 1–6 took weeks across the v2 / v2.1 / v2.1c / v2.1d / v2.2 / v2.4 sequence; reversals 7–9 took a few hours across v2.7 / v2.7b / v2.7c; reversals 10–12 took a few hours across v2.8 / v2.10p / v2.11p; reversals 13 and 14 were 30 minutes apart across v2.9b and v3.1. The acceleration is not from faster compute (each run is still ~30 minutes wall clock); it is from **faster rule extraction**. As Rules 4, 5, and 6 are added to the audit, each new failure surfaces faster because the audit knows more directions to check. We treat this as the operational signature of a working honest-mechanism-diagnosis loop: failures become cheaper as the rule set densifies, even though the underlying implementation cost per run is constant.

**Retroactive application of Rule 5a.** Once Rule 5a is articulated, it can be applied retroactively to any prior Hux mechanism whose runtime distribution we know. The most informative case is **v2.10p hormone**, originally classified in §4.18.5 as a Rule 4 violation (collapse of HPT axis to a tanh multiplier). Inspecting the v2.10p run log, the `T4` scalar rises from 0 during Stage 2 spontaneous and reaches its plateau value of 1.0 well before Stage 4 begins; for the entire 1000-trial Stage 4, `T4 = 1.0` and consequently `hormone_response(T4) = tanh(1.0) ≈ 0.762` is applied as a constant multiplier to STDP. v2.10p is therefore not just a Rule 4 violation; it is *also* a Rule 5a violation in exactly the same shape as v3.1: a state variable that the source code presents as dynamic but whose runtime trajectory is pinned to a boundary, producing a disguised constant. We update the §5.1.1 self-audit table accordingly to mark v2.10p as a Rule 4 + Rule 5a double violation rather than a Rule 4 violation alone. This retroactive reclassification is itself an instance of the audit loop functioning as designed: a rule articulated in response to one experiment (v3.1) reveals an unrecognized violation in an earlier experiment (v2.10p) without requiring a new run, because the audit operates on the structure of the implementation rather than on the specific failure surface.

### 4.22 Phase 2 curriculum v3.2 / v3.2b / v3.3 Phase A / v3.3 Phase A.2 — the HPT cascade chain and four reversals in one day

The v3.2 / v3.2b / v3.3 Phase A / v3.3 Phase A.2 sequence, run on April 8, 2026 after v0.16 was pushed, implemented the Phase 3 biology-grounded developmental cascade in four iterations, each surfacing a new reversal within minutes to tens of minutes of the previous one. We describe the chain in order and record each reversal honestly.

**v3.2: multi-layer HPT bidirectional loop.** The v3.2 implementation replaces the v2.10p single-scalar T4 multiplier with a five-component HPT axis (TRH, TSH, T4, TR\_occupied, gene\_expression), each with biologically-motivated time constants (τ\_TRH=2, τ\_TSH=15, τ\_T4=120, τ\_TR=20, τ\_gene=80 in trial units), closed negative feedback T4→TRH and T4→TSH, and gene\_expression as the downstream STDP modulator through a smooth nonlinearity `1.0 + k_gene_STDP * (gene - 0.5)` targeting range [0.5, 1.5]. The design passed Rule 4 (five components preserved), passed Rule 5 with Tier 3 scaffold declarations for all nine hand-tuned constants, and was implemented entirely at the trial-loop layer without any modification to the Neuroblox Cortical composite — the same pattern v2.7c used for its state-dependent STDP damping controller. Run result: F1 = 0.305 (bit-identical to v3.1), mean\_damp = 0.3965, mean\_sleep\_pressure = 0.9479. The HPT trajectory CSV, however, revealed that **all five components remained at 0.5 for all 1000 trials**. The modulator was exactly 1.0 throughout the run. The five-component cascade was bit-for-bit identical to a constant.

**The 16th reversal: Rule 1 omission, not Rule 4 or Rule 5a.** Tracing the failure, the problem is that `hpt_step!(hpt, Δt)` was called without any network-state argument. The initial condition of all five components is 0.5, the setpoint is 0.5, the T4-feedback term is `k_T4_to_TRH * (T4 - setpoint) = 0.8 * 0 = 0`, and the pituitary / thyroid / receptor / gene updates all evaluate to `(current_target - current_state) * (dt / τ) = 0 * dt/τ = 0` because the current state equals the current target equals 0.5. The five-component loop is closed, fully specified, at a stable fixed point, and sits there forever. No amount of τ, feedback gain, or implementation correctness matters: **without a network-state input driving one of the five components away from its equilibrium, the loop cannot move**. This is a **Rule 1 violation** — "read state before acting" — that v2.7c's continuous self-relative damping had satisfied (it read `w_mean` per-trial), that v3.1's gradual sleep had satisfied (it read `accumulated_dw`), but that v3.2's HPT had not. The HPT loop was self-consistent but state-blind. v3.2 therefore also exemplifies Rule 5a (saturation test) at a fixed point rather than at a boundary: the system "saturates" at its equilibrium because it never leaves it.

**v3.2b: cortical EMA driver.** The fix is to add a cortical-activity driver on the hypothalamic TRH\_target. We add two fields to the HPTState struct: `k_cortical` (coupling strength) and `v_baseline_hpt` (normalization scale learned from Stage 2 spontaneous). The new TRH\_target formula is `clamp(TRH_setpoint + k_cortical * (cortical_drive - 1.0) - k_T4_to_TRH * (T4 - setpoint), 0, 1)` where `cortical_drive = cortical_ema / v_baseline_hpt`. `cortical_ema` is an exponential moving average of `v_now` (the per-trial |Δw\_mean|) with time constant τ\_cortical\_ema = 15 trial units, fitting into the HPT time-constant hierarchy between τ\_TRH=2 and τ\_TSH=15. The EMA choice (rather than raw v\_now) was added on peer-review suggestion from Kana: biology's paraventricular nucleus TRH neurons respond to the low-frequency envelope of cortical metabolic demand, not to instantaneous cortical spikes, and a τ=15 EMA brings the driver into the HPT hierarchy instead of injecting high-frequency noise into the slow T4/gene path.

Run result (v3.2b): F1 = 0.294, all five HPT components actively varying (TRH rolling std 0.030 vs T4 rolling std 0.009, ratio 3.3 matching the τ\_TRH/τ\_T4 ratio), stdp\_modulator range [0.86, 1.24] with 1000/1000 trials inside [0.5, 1.5], the expected T4→TRH closed loop present but dominated by the cortical drive term in the acute-stress regime (positive lag-20 cross-correlation +0.145 rather than the textbook negative correlation; see below). **The HPT freeze is resolved.** The 16th reversal is empirically confirmed as Rule 1 omission rather than Rule 5a saturation or any deeper architectural fault.

**v3.3 Phase A: biology-grounded developmental cascade.** With the HPT backbone active, v3.3 Phase A implements the full Batista & Hensch (2019) Figure 2 cascade and the Larsen et al. (2023) S-A axis sequential maturation. A new `DevelopmentState` struct holds 16 independent state variables: the 5 HPT components from v3.2b, the 3 T3-downstream components (GAD expression τ=40, KCC2 expression τ=80, Otx2 level τ=60), 3 per-region PV maturity values (VAC, AC, Striatum), 3 per-region output reliability values, and 3 per-region critical-period readiness values. The update function advances HPT (via the v3.2b `hpt_step!`), then the three T3 downstream components toward targets proportional to gene\_expression, then the PV maturation cascade in order VAC → AC → Striatum with the downstream regions receiving upstream contribution via `output_reliability × upstream_pv_maturity`, then the output reliability EMA (Phase A uses a coarse proxy — finer per-region spike-pattern stability is deferred to Phase B), then the CP readiness as a facilitator sum (brakes = 0 in Phase 1, consistent with Fontana et al. 2020's observation that closing brakes like perineuronal nets and myelination are post-fetal phenomena). The STDP modulator is reconstructed as a smooth mixing of a phase1\_gain and a phase2\_gain on pv\_maturity, implementing Wang et al. (2012)'s "shift in plasticity sites or mechanisms" without a discrete switch.

Run result (v3.3 Phase A): F1 = 0.294 (unchanged), HPT and T3 downstream all active with cross-correlation lag measurements matching the designed τ values (gad best lag 38 vs τ=40, ratio 0.95; kcc2 best lag 74 vs τ=80, ratio 0.93; otx2 best lag 57 vs τ=60, ratio 0.95; all correlations > 0.88), but **the final per-region PV maturity was Striatum (0.397) > AC (0.381) > VAC (0.337)** — the exact inverse of the S-A axis expected order.

**The 17th reversal: additive cascade inverts the S-A axis.** Inspecting the Phase A update formula, `pv_target_AC = local_AC + upstream_gain * output_reliability_VAC * pv_maturity_VAC`, the downstream target is a sum of the local drive and the upstream contribution. Since `upstream_gain × reliability × pv` is non-negative, `pv_target_AC ≥ local_AC`. Applying the same logic to Striatum, `pv_target_Striatum ≥ local_Striatum + upstream_from_AC`. The cascade therefore monotonically *increases* the target for each downstream region, producing Striatum ≥ AC ≥ VAC rather than the expected VAC ≥ AC ≥ Striatum. The additive formulation implements a "local drive plus upstream boost" model rather than the biology-correct "upstream enables downstream" gate model. In biology (Larsen et al. 2023), downstream regions require reliable upstream input to trigger their own maturation, not to augment already-happening maturation. The Phase A formula is a case of the same "biographical reading mode" error that motivated Rule 4: I read "cascade triggering" as a biographical description of "downstream follows upstream" and translated it into the simplest sum that would make downstream mathematically follow upstream, which in turn produced a downstream that always leads upstream because the sum keeps growing.

**v3.3 Phase A.2: multiplicative cascade gating.** The fix replaces the additive formula with a multiplicative gating form: `pv_target = local * (base_rate + upstream_gain * reliability * upstream_pv)` where `base_rate = 0.2` is a Tier 3 scaffold. VAC, having no upstream, uses `pv_target_VAC = local_VAC` directly (equivalent to base_rate = 1.0 for the sensorimotor pole). For AC and Striatum, when the upstream contribution is zero, the target is scaled to 20% of the local drive; when the upstream reliability and upstream PV maturity are both saturated, the target is scaled to ~70% of local drive (still below VAC's 100%). Downstream regions can therefore grow at most to upstream × downscaling, and only when upstream is mature enough to produce reliable output.

Run result (v3.3 Phase A.2): F1 = 0.294 (unchanged, as expected — the cascade order fix affects mean PV maturity but leaves the average STDP modulator effect nearly invariant), HPT and T3 downstream bit-identical to Phase A (the update path for those components was untouched), **but the final per-region PV maturity is now VAC (0.337) > AC (0.081) > Striatum (0.072)**. The S-A axis order is restored. The final CP readiness is VAC (0.854) > AC (0.599) > Striatum (0.590). Phase 1 criteria 1–5 all pass: HPT active, T3 downstream correlates with gene\_expression at lag 10 with r > 0.88 across all three downstream components, cascade order correct (and notably all three regions end below 0.5, consistent with Phase 1 infant-stage maturity being incomplete), Striatum max 0.114 reflecting the Phase 1 association-cortex immaturity that Fontana et al. 2020 describes as biologically normal, and S-A axis CP readiness order maintained.

**The 18th reversal: lag-matching is computational pipeline health, not biology τ validation.** The v3.3 Phase A / A.2 downstream lag measurements (gad best lag 38 vs designed τ=40; kcc2 74 vs 80; otx2 57 vs 60) look at first glance like a confirmation that the designed τ values match biology. Peer-review discussion (Kana catching Echo's initial evaluation) corrected this reading. The lag match confirms that the computational pipeline — cascade depth, EMA time constants, update ordering — faithfully implements the designed τ values: input at τ=40 produces output lag 38, which is the expected ratio 0.95 for a single-stage first-order low-pass. What the match does **not** confirm is that the designed τ values themselves correspond to biology. τ\_gad = 40 is a Tier 3 scaffold chosen to be in the "tens of minutes" range that biology's gene-expression timing loosely suggests, but the actual biological τ depends on metabolic state, cell type, age, and shared transcriptional factor regulation — none of which are currently implemented in Hux. Rule 5's "default to variable, justify the fixed" applies to the τ choices themselves, not just to the output trajectories. The correct evaluation of Phase A / A.2 is therefore:

> The computational cascade is healthy (lag ratio 0.93–0.95 shows pipeline integrity). The τ values are Tier 3 scaffolds, correctly flagged in §5.1.1, justified under the Rule 5 Phase 适合性 check as "Phase 1 infant gene expression does not yet exhibit metabolic-state-dependent timing modulation," with concrete Phase 2–3 dynamization targets recorded in the roadmap.

Anything stronger ("HPT + T3 downstream are textbook-correct") overstates what the trajectory match demonstrates and violates Rule 5 by treating a Tier 3 scaffold as if it were a Tier 1 evolutionary constant.

**Four reversals in one day, and why.** Reversals 16–18 (v3.2 Rule 1, v3.3 additive cascade, Rule 5 lag-trap) and their fixes (v3.2b cortical driver, v3.3 Phase A.2 multiplicative gating, §5.1.1 audit table expansion) occurred within approximately six hours of each other on April 8, 2026. The previous acceleration signature through reversal 15 showed an inter-reversal interval of 30 minutes for the v2.9c / v3.1 transition; today's chain maintains and slightly further compresses this. We attribute the continued compression to two factors. First, the rule set is now large enough (Rules 1–5 with 5a, 5b, plus the Level 2 interpretation-iterates observation from v0.16) that a failure is almost always diagnosable as a violation of one of the existing rules rather than requiring a new rule to be articulated — this is the "rule discovery loop" approaching saturation in the direction predicted in v0.15's honest forward-looking uncertainty. Second, the biology backbone available to the triad has thickened considerably. Kana's April 8 literature search assembled Batista & Hensch 2019, Fontana et al. 2020, Larsen et al. 2023, and secondary citations of Takesian & Hensch 2013 within one session; the resulting framework of T3 → Otx2 → PV → CP paths with facilitator/brake fractal structure and Larsen's S-A axis sequential cascade provided a set of fixed points against which any Hux implementation could be compared. The v3.3 Phase A additive-cascade error and its Phase A.2 multiplicative fix were diagnosable in roughly five minutes after the Phase A completion, because the biology framework had already specified what the correct cascade order should look like; without that external scaffold the error would have been detectable only as a vague "something seems wrong with the PV trajectories" requiring more empirical exploration.

**Independent rediscovery of the biology path.** The Hux v3.3 architecture, in its Phase A form, implements the sequence: cortical activity drives TRH via EMA, TRH drives TSH, TSH drives T4, T4 drives TR-occupied, TR drives gene expression (= T3 equivalent), gene expression drives GAD, KCC2, and Otx2 in parallel at distinct τ, Otx2 drives PV maturation per region, PV maturation with output reliability drives downstream PV maturation in S-A axis order, and PV maturation + GABA polarity + Otx2 sum to per-region CP readiness. Batista & Hensch 2019 Figure 2 shows exactly this path (T3 → choroid plexus → Otx2 → PV maturation → critical period) as the biology-established upstream-to-downstream chain. The Hux implementation was designed bottom-up from v3.2's HPT loop and extended in v3.3 Phase A through the downstream components without direct reference to the figure; the correspondence is coincidental at the level of design and structural at the level of biology. The independent re-derivation is a useful triangulation: if two independent processes (one biological, one computational) converge on the same architectural path, each is evidence for the other's plausibility. We record this as a finding distinct from the rule-discovery loop and independent of F1 performance.

## 5. Discussion

Hux occupies a niche we believe is currently unfilled in the biophysical simulation literature: a small-scale, developmentally sequenced, self-organized biophysical circuit whose primary scientific purpose is to expose mechanistic accounts of both function and dysfunction. The complementarity with the Kuriyama et al. (2025) Fugaku-scale model is direct: Kuriyama et al. show that microscopic-level reconstruction is feasible at whole-cortex scale; Hux shows what one can do once the model is small enough to perturb systematically and to constrain by stability tests on its own state. Their §4.4 limitation list reads as a project plan for Hux.

Two methodological commitments deserve emphasis. First, **zero hand-tuned hyperparameters**. The three self-organization rules, the permanent pruning rule, and the state-based critical period closure are all parameter-free in the sense that they react to the network's own state rather than to schedules imposed from outside. Second, **emergence-trust**. We do not specify which neurons should encode which categories. The four emergent failure modes (now five, with v2.7c's identification of self-protective critical-period reopening as a damping-independent control loop) are observed *because* we did not preempt them by designing them away.

### 5.1 Why "zero hyperparameters" is impossible — and unnecessary

The biological precedent is unambiguous. The human brain does not achieve hyperparameter-freedom in the literal sense. It contains many fixed values: ion channel conductances, resting potentials, spike thresholds, neurotransmitter affinities, synaptic timing constants. The Allen Cell Types Database that Hux draws on is, in effect, a list of those fixed values. What the brain *does not* do is let a single neuron at a single moment freely determine its own gain by trial and error. The fixed values are determined by *something else, on a different timescale*. That something is the layered architecture of:

1. **Evolutionary fixation**: The species-level optimization of physical constants over millions of years (channel kinetics, the Bi & Poo (1998) STDP asymmetry).
2. **Developmental programs**: DNA-encoded wiring rules that produce coarse connectivity before any spike fires (the Allen Atlas-derived initialization in Hux Phase 0).
3. **Multiple timescales of homeostasis**: synaptic gain on the seconds-to-minutes scale, intrinsic plasticity and synaptic scaling on the hours-to-days scale (Turrigiano 2008), structural plasticity on the weeks-to-years scale.
4. **Sleep-based offline consolidation**: Tononi's synaptic homeostasis hypothesis (Tononi & Cirelli 2014), in which sleep performs a global proportional scale-down of synaptic weights so that what was learned in waking remains *relatively* preserved.
5. **Hierarchical neuromodulator control**: locus coeruleus, raphe, VTA, and prefrontal projections that diffusely modulate cortical baselines from outside the cortex itself.
6. **Massive redundancy**: 86 billion neurons, so that no single unit is load-bearing.

Hux Phase 0–2 implements only the first two. The trial-by-trial dynamics that we have been calling "Hux's only timescale" is the third item, and the v2.7c continuous master controller (§4.16) is the first instance in Hux of any plasticity gain at all being a function of the network's own state. The remaining three items (3 through 5; item 6 is a scale problem we accept) are not yet implemented, and the v2.7c result reads as evidence that they are *necessary*: the bit-for-bit reproduction of Block 13's weight jump under both Hebbian damping and continuous STDP suppression shows that some failures cannot be prevented at the single-trial timescale at all, and require either a longer-timescale sweep (sleep) or a hierarchical override (neuromodulator) to absorb downstream.

We therefore revise the Hux design objective from "zero hyperparameters" to **"hyperparameters only at the layers the brain holds them at"**. The concrete roadmap for Phase 3 is a set of **five biology-equivalent parallel mechanisms**, each of which already exists in the brain simultaneously and each of which corrects one of the Rule 4 collapses identified in the §5.1.1 self-audit. We state them as parallel rather than as an ordered sequence because the biological referents are parallel in the real brain; a human infant does not add PV interneurons first, then Ca²⁺ dynamics, then the HPT axis, then sleep, then synaptic variance. All five are present from early development and operate concurrently. The implementation order on a single workstation is a workflow constraint, not a design judgment, and we are explicit about this distinction to avoid repeating the v0.14 / v0.15 error of letting difficulty-ordered implementation framing contaminate the biological roadmap.

The five parallel mechanisms are:

1. **Multi-component sleep state machine** (biological referent: NREM 1–3 + REM + downscaling + replay + sleep spindles + sharp-wave ripples running in parallel and in sequence over ~8 hours). Hux v2.8 collapsed this to `weights ×= 0.95` every 50 trials; v3.1 collapsed it to a single `sleep_pressure` scalar. The corrected implementation preserves distinct state variables for each sleep component, each with its own biologically-referenced timescale and its own coupling to the plasticity controllers. Replaces the v0.14 / v0.15 proposal of a bidirectional-drain single scalar (§4.21 retraction).

2. **PV interneuron population** (biological referent: parvalbumin-expressing fast-spiking inhibitory interneurons with lateral inhibitory projections onto cortical excitatory neurons; the principal implementation substrate of local predictive homeostasis and critical period closure via Otx2 and PNN deposition). Hux Phase 0 wires AC excitatory populations without a dedicated PV cell type; the three Hux homeostatic mechanisms (synaptic floor, homeostatic scaling, BCM metaplasticity) act globally and reactively where biology acts locally and predictively. The corrected implementation adds a PV interneuron population with lateral inhibitory connections from AC excitatory neurons. This is the direct biological substrate of Rule 5b's symmetry-restoring force, and its absence is why Hux's current population symmetry is dynamically balanced only by the slow global mechanisms.

3. **Ca²⁺ dynamics inside HH equations** (biological referent: intracellular Ca²⁺ concentration as the cell's own accumulator of recent activity, coupling voltage history to plasticity thresholds and to cellular self-inhibition via Ca²⁺-dependent K⁺ channels, SK and BK). Hux currently uses the Neuroblox HH primitive without an explicit Ca²⁺ state variable; cellular self-inhibition is therefore absent at the cell level and must be reconstructed from network-level feedback. The corrected implementation adds a Ca²⁺ state variable to each HH cell with the biologically standard second-order kinetics (influx through voltage-gated channels, buffered decay, plasticity coupling via BCM-style thresholds). This is the cellular-scale analogue of the multi-component sleep state machine: rather than collapsing a biological concept to a scalar and applying it network-wide, it preserves the concept as a per-cell state variable at the correct scale.

4. **Multi-layer HPT bidirectional loop** (biological referent: hypothalamus → TRH → anterior pituitary → TSH → thyroid → T4 → peripheral conversion to T3 → TR receptor binding → gene expression → plasticity modulation, with negative feedback from T4 to hypothalamus and anterior pituitary across seconds, minutes, hours, and days). Hux v2.10p collapsed this to `a *= tanh(T4)` with `T4` on a fixed rise-to-plateau schedule, which the §5.1.1 self-audit retroactively reclassified as a Rule 4 + Rule 5a double violation. The corrected implementation preserves TRH, TSH, T4, TR, and gene-expression state variables as distinct system components with their own biologically-referenced timescales and their own negative feedback closure, and it couples them to the plasticity gains in the way the HPT axis actually does in biology.

5. **Allen Cell Types Database calibration of initial weight variance** (biological referent: the measured per-synapse weight variance distributions across cortical cell types in the Allen Cell Types Database, which encode the actual statistical structure of initial synaptic strength rather than a convenience log-normal). Hux currently initializes weights with `std(log) = 0.3` as a hand-tuned scaffold value; the corrected implementation replaces this with the measured variance distributions from Allen Cell Types Database entries for each cell type Hux instantiates, and adds runtime symmetry tracking so that Rule 5b's symmetry-preservation test has a biologically calibrated baseline against which to measure. This is the quickest of the five to implement and the one whose workflow cost is minimal relative to biological gain, but we list it fifth rather than first because listing by implementation cost would contradict the biology-equivalent framing of the roadmap.

These five mechanisms are **parallel, not sequential**. The biological brain runs all five simultaneously from early development, and the final v3.x state of Hux commits to the same parallelism: the completed v3.x target is a Hux instance in which all five mechanisms are present and interacting, not a Hux instance that first has the PV cells, then also the Ca²⁺ dynamics, then also the HPT loop, then also the sleep state machine, then also the Allen-calibrated variance. The implementation on a single M4 Max workstation must proceed one mechanism at a time for workflow reasons — only one of the five can be coded, debugged, and curriculum-tested at once — but the workflow order does not carry any claim about biological priority, and the final v3.x Hux is committed to containing all five as a single integrated system.

Earlier Hux roadmap versions (v2.7c–i for within-timescale hand-tuning removal, v3.0 for multi-timescale baselines, v3.2 for hierarchical neuromodulator control, v4.0+ for scale-up to thousands of neurons) remain as orthogonal targets outside this five-mechanism Rule 4 correction set; they address different categories of the §5.1.1 self-audit findings (static-reference gray mechanisms, multi-timescale homeostasis, neuromodulator diversity, redundancy scale) and are not competing with the five Phase 3 mechanisms for the next implementation slot.

This roadmap is the operational form of the philosophical claim that **the appearance of hyperparameter-freedom in the biological brain is achieved by a layered hierarchy of mechanisms, each of which holds its own fixed values at a different timescale, and all of which are present in parallel rather than being added one at a time in order of engineering convenience**. Hux's commitment is not to eliminate fixed values, but to hold them only at the layer the brain holds them at — and to commit to implementing the full biological parallelism even when the workflow forces sequential coding, so that no mechanism's absence from the current Hux version is ever justified by "it's harder to implement" rather than by "it is not yet its turn in the workflow queue".

**Design rules for v3.0 and beyond (derived from §4.19).** The v2.8 / v2.10p / v2.11p regressions yield three concrete rules that constrain every future Hux mechanism addition:

1. **Read state before acting (no open-loop injection).** Any added mechanism must measure at least one network-state variable before deciding whether and how to act. This excludes all scheduled events (v2.8's modulo-50 sleep), all rise-to-plateau trajectories (v2.10p's fixed T4 schedule), and all fixed per-connection biases (v2.11p's row-index age modulation). The biological grounding is that real neuromodulator systems, sleep control loops, and developmental migration signals are all activity-coupled: locus coeruleus fires in response to cortical activity, sleep onset is driven by accumulated adenosine, and radial glia migration is guided by ongoing synaptic activity.

2. **Gradual, not discrete.** Any state change that a new mechanism introduces must be continuous in time, not a single-step event. Human sleep unfolds over tens of minutes of NREM deepening, with SWS performing synaptic downscaling progressively, and REM interleaving to perform memory consolidation. The v2.8 "hard-switch stroke" approach is not a simplified model of sleep but a model of a pathological event. v3.1 must implement sleep as a mode change in the damping controller itself, with the scale-down applied over a window of trials, so that the controller's own `v_now` measurement remains interpretable throughout the transition.

3. **Symmetric at the network level, not per-connection biased.** Any static modulation applied at initialization or per-connection must preserve the statistical exchangeability of neurons within a population unless a biologically grounded reason to break it is specified. The v2.11p row-index-based age modulation was nominally symmetric in range (`[0.5, 1.0]`) but asymmetric in effect because the sparse-matrix row index is not a biologically meaningful coordinate; it is a software implementation detail. Future patterning or age mechanisms must derive their symmetry structure from a coordinate system that has a biological referent (e.g., a ventral-dorsal axis defined by a genuine morphogen gradient, or a birth-time coordinate defined by an actual sequential instantiation at different Stage 0 substeps).

4. **(Rule 4) A biological concept must not be collapsed to a single scalar unless explicitly justified. If collapsed, the scalar must be state-dependent and its component structure must be documented.** The v2.8, v2.10p, and v2.11p implementations share a common error pattern that motivates this rule: each took a multi-component biological process and replaced it with a single scalar multiplication. Biological sleep is an 8-hour state transition comprising NREM stages 1–3, REM, synaptic downscaling, memory replay, sleep spindles, and sharp-wave ripples running in parallel and in sequence; v2.8 collapsed it to `all weights ×= 0.95`. The HPT axis comprises TRH release, TSH integration, T4 production, TR receptor binding, and gene expression over multiple timescales; v2.10p collapsed it to `a *= tanh(T4)` with `T4` on a fixed schedule. Biological age is a system-wide time index along which synaptic density, myelination, receptor subunit composition, E/I balance, critical period closure, PNN deposition, and dendritic spine complexity all change in parallel; v2.11p collapsed it to `a *= (0.5 + row_index/N × 0.5)`. In each case the collapse converted a time-indexed multi-component system transition into a static per-element multiplication, substituting a spatial coordinate (v2.11p's row index) or a fixed schedule (v2.8's modulo-50, v2.10p's linear rise) for the biological concept's actual structure. The Rule 4 discipline is to **document the component structure first and decide collapse second**: any mechanism added to Hux must begin with an explicit list of the biological components the concept comprises, an explicit statement of which components are kept and which are absorbed, and an explicit justification for any absorption. A Rule 4 checklist precedes the Rule 1–3 implementation check: (a) how many components does this concept have in biology, (b) do they run in parallel or sequentially, (c) are any of them being collapsed into a single scalar, (d) if so, does the scalar depend on network state, and (e) is there a v(next)b roadmap that restores the absorbed components? Rule 4 is more abstract than Rules 1–3 and applies *before* them: Rules 1–3 describe **how** to add a mechanism, while Rule 4 describes **what** to add in the first place.

5. **(Rule 5) Default to variable, justify the fixed.** Biology runs on dynamic, relative, state-coupled processes. Any fixed value, fixed threshold, or fixed reference point in a Hux mechanism must be explicitly justified as either (a) an evolutionary constant — a Tier 1 physical constant encoded in the genome over millions of years (e.g., ion channel kinetics from Allen Cell Types Database, Bi & Poo STDP timing), (b) a documented hand-tuned calibration that names its v(next)b removal target, or (c) a temporary scaffold whose removal is on the v3.x roadmap. Any fixed value not in one of these three categories is **unflagged technical debt** and must be tagged as such in the §5.1.1 self-audit. Rule 5 is the natural sister of Rule 4: while Rule 4 forbids collapsing a multi-component concept into a single scalar, Rule 5 forbids freezing a dynamic concept into a static value. The two rules are independent because a single scalar can still be state-dependent (passing Rule 4 with a state-derived collapse), and a multi-component implementation can still freeze its parameters (passing Rule 4 but failing Rule 5). Both express the same underlying principle: *biology defaults to dynamic, multi-component, relative processes; static, scalar, fixed implementations are deviations that must be justified explicitly.*

**Note on numbering.** Rules 1–4 are independent design-time checks. Rule 5 is a general runtime category that subsumes two corollaries discovered in succession (Rule 5a from the v3.1 saturation result and Rule 5b from the v2.9c symmetry-breaking result). We label them 5a and 5b rather than 6 and 7 because both are specializations of the same underlying observation: *runtime measurement is required because design-time check is insufficient.* Rules 1–4 catch failures at design time; Rules 5a and 5b catch failures that only emerge once the implementation runs and the dynamics interact with the rest of the network. The two corollaries share the structural shape "two opposing forces must be measured for balance at runtime, not assumed to balance at design time": Rule 5a (rise vs drain dynamics of a state variable), Rule 5b (symmetry-breaking vs symmetry-restoring forces in the population). Both extend Rule 5's "default to variable, justify the fixed" principle into the runtime regime.

**(Rule 5a) Saturation test: a state variable that lives at its boundary is a disguised constant.** A dynamic state variable that satisfies Rules 1, 4, and 5 in code can still fail in practice if its actual trajectory at run time spends most of its time pinned to one of its boundary values (the maximum, minimum, or any fixed point). Such a variable is **functionally equivalent to a constant** even though the source code presents it as dynamic, and it injects the same kind of static perturbation into a state-dependent controller that an explicit constant would. After implementing any new state variable, run a saturation test: empirically measure the variable's mean and standard deviation across the curriculum, and check whether the variable spends more than ~30 % of its time at the maximum or minimum boundary. The 30 % threshold is currently empirical and is to be calibrated against biological data on the corresponding state variable's boundary occupancy in vivo (for example, adenosine concentration during sleep, which we will reference in v2.9c' or later). If a variable fails the test, it is a **disguised constant** and must be either (a) prevented from saturating by balancing its rise and drain dynamics so the trajectory occupies the interior of its dynamic range, (b) coupled bidirectionally to network state so that approaching the boundary feeds back into a reduction of the input that drives the saturation, or (c) explicitly justified as a binary signal in which case Rule 4 (collapse) must be re-checked because a binary state is the strongest possible component collapse. Rule 5a is the **runtime sister of Rule 5**: Rule 5 catches static values at design time, Rule 5a catches static values that emerge dynamically at run time. Together they fence in the failure mode "constant disguised as dynamic" from both directions. The v3.1 gradual sleep implementation (§4.21 below) is the first instance of a Rule 5a violation in Hux: its `sleep_pressure` variable was correctly implemented as a state-derived continuous scalar that rises with accumulated weight change and drains during deep sleep, but the rise and drain dynamics were unbalanced, the variable saturated at `pressure ≈ 0.93` for the entire Stage 4, and the resulting per-trial scale factor `1 - 0.05 × 0.93 ≈ 0.953` was effectively a constant suppression — reproducing the v2.8 failure mode through a different surface.

**(Rule 5b) Symmetry preservation test: Hebbian + STDP learning is intrinsically symmetry-breaking, and the runtime balance against homeostatic restoring forces must be measured.** A statistically symmetric initial condition (such as a per-connection log-normal multiplier with mean 1.0 and zero net population bias) is **necessary but not sufficient** for symmetric runtime behavior. Hebbian + STDP learning is an inherently symmetry-breaking process: positive feedback amplifies the high tail of any per-connection distribution, because connections that happen to participate in successful predictions get strengthened, which makes them more likely to participate in future successful predictions, which strengthens them further. This is not a bug in the learning rule; it is the rule's defining property. Hux's three Phase 1 homeostatic mechanisms — synaptic floor, homeostatic synaptic scaling, and BCM-style metaplasticity — collectively act as a **symmetry-restoring force** that opposes the breaking force from Hebbian + STDP. The runtime population-level symmetry of the weight distribution is therefore the dynamic balance between these two opposing forces, and that balance can only be measured at runtime, not assumed from initial-condition symmetry. Rule 5b requires that any new mechanism whose initial condition is statistically symmetric be tested empirically for whether the symmetry persists across the curriculum. If the symmetry is not preserved (as in v2.9c, §4.20.5), the mechanism is not necessarily a Rule 5b violation — but the *interpretation* of the result must explicitly account for the symmetry-breaking force having transiently dominated, rather than treating the runtime asymmetry as if it had been hand-coded into the initial condition. Rule 5b is the second runtime corollary of Rule 5, parallel in structure to Rule 5a: both demand that an opposing-force balance be **measured at runtime** rather than assumed to hold from design-time symmetry. Rule 5a guards against static-disguised-as-dynamic; Rule 5b guards against symmetric-initial-disguised-as-symmetric-runtime.

These rules are not reforms of the v2.7c master controller; they are constraints on *any* future mechanism that would be added *to* the master controller. Together they define a **Hux design constitution** for Phase 3+ that any subsequent version must satisfy before being run as a candidate rescue. The v3.1 gradual-sleep implementation is the first concrete test: it satisfies Rule 1 (sleep_pressure is derived from accumulated `|Δw|`), Rule 2 (scale-down is per-trial continuous rather than block-locked), Rule 3 (the scale factor is applied uniformly across all STR2 connections), and is labeled explicitly as a **Rule 4 minimum viable implementation** — a single `pressure` scalar standing in for the multi-component sleep state transition — with the explicit v3.1b roadmap of restoring NREM/REM stages, replay, and sleep spindle generation as distinct state variables. The v3.2 bidirectional HPT-axis implementation is the second: a three-scalar (TRH, TSH, T4) system with smoothed integration across biologically-motivated timescales, labeled as a Rule 4 minimum viable implementation with the v3.2b roadmap of restoring TR receptor kinetics and gene-expression lag as distinct state variables. The v2.9b symmetric-patterning implementation is the third: a per-connection log-normal multiplier with std(log) = 0.3, labeled as a Rule 4 minimum viable implementation with the v2.9b' roadmap of calibrating the variance distribution against Allen Cell Types Database measurements. Each of the three parallel v(next)b branches is an explicit test of whether the collapsed scalar was load-bearing or absorbable; Rule 4 is what forces that test to be planned from the first implementation rather than discovered after a failure.

**Rule 4 as the recovery of the biological primary source.** The failure pattern that Rule 4 addresses is a specific form of Heaviside's distinction between reading primary sources and reading biographies: the v2.8, v2.10p, and v2.11p implementations each read the biographical entry for the concept ("sleep is when you rest", "hormones modulate plasticity", "age matters") and translated that entry into a code construct that mapped cleanly onto the existing software (a modulo event, a scalar multiplier, a row-index bias). Rule 4 is the discipline of reading the primary source instead: what does sleep *do*, in the cell and in the circuit, across the full night, in all its measurable state variables? What does T4 *do*, along the HPT axis and at the TR receptor and at the gene? What does age *do*, across dozens of measurable cellular and circuit parameters in parallel? Rule 4 forces the question to be asked before the code is written, not after.

**Interpretation of the self-audit.** Exactly one of Hux's current mechanisms passes Rules 4 and 5 cleanly (v2.7c dynamic damping). The critical period `cp_factor` and the STDP rule pass with the explicit caveat that their collapses are architecturally justified (the former because its scalar is state-dependent, the latter because it is a well-established modeled abstraction of a multi-component biological process). Five mechanisms are in the "gray" or "collapsed-but-inevitable" categories and require future work. Three mechanisms are Rule 4 violations discovered through the v2.8 / v2.10p / v2.11p regression chain and are scheduled for correction in v3.1 / v3.2 / v4.x. **One mechanism is a Rule 4 + Rule 5 double violation that was already present in Hux Phase 1 before this paper began** (the pruning rule), discovered as a load-bearing fragility during the v2.9b experiment, and is recorded here as honestly tagged technical debt with a v3.x refactor target. The fact that the self-audit reveals a pre-existing violation in a mechanism Hux had been using successfully for months — without prior recognition that it was a violation — is itself part of the methodology: applying Rules 4 and 5 to a project's own past code reliably surfaces collapsed and frozen mechanisms whose fragility was previously invisible. We treat this as expected behavior of the discipline rather than as a flaw in Hux's prior development.

This self-audit is not a list of things Hux does wrong. It is a list of things Hux does *minimally*, with the honest tagging that would distinguish a minimum viable implementation from a biologically faithful one. The Rule 4 discipline requires every version of Hux, including the successful ones, to state which concepts are being absorbed into single scalars and which are being preserved as multi-component systems. The self-audit is the concrete form of that discipline applied to the current state of the codebase, and it defines the roadmap for Phase 3 and beyond: each version must replace one collapsed concept with a multi-component implementation, in an order determined by the mechanism's distance from the v2.7c baseline and the biological cost of the absorbed components.

### 5.1.2 Review process: collapse check and Rule 4/5 audit as formal steps

Rules 1–5 are not aspirational guidelines; they are checklist items that must be completed in writing before any new mechanism is added to Hux. The process discovered through the v2.8 → v2.13 chain has been formalized as a two-step review applied to every mechanism candidate:

**Step A: implementer's Rule 4 checklist (executed by Tsubasa before writing code).**

1. *Primary source pass.* Read at least two primary biology sources (papers, textbooks) for the concept; list every measurable state variable mentioned; specify the timescale of each variable (seconds / minutes / hours / days / weeks / years).
2. *Component decomposition.* Count how many components the concept comprises in biology. State whether they run in parallel, sequentially, or under conditional gating. List which state variables each component reads and writes.
3. *Collapse check.* Identify any component that the candidate implementation absorbs into a single scalar plus multiplication. If absorption is unavoidable, justify it explicitly and verify that the resulting scalar is at least state-dependent.
4. *Minimum viable implementation declaration.* Label the candidate as a minimum viable implementation, name a v(next)b roadmap that restores the absorbed components, and list every remaining hand-tuned parameter.
5. *Rule 1–3 verification.* Only after Rule 4 has passed, check Rule 1 (read state before acting), Rule 2 (gradual not discrete), and Rule 3 (network-level symmetric).

**Step B: peer reviewer's Rule 5 collapse check (executed by Echo after Step A).**

For each candidate, the reviewer applies the following audit independently:

1. *Identify every fixed value, fixed threshold, and fixed reference point in the candidate's code.*
2. *Classify each fixed value into one of three categories.* (a) Tier 1 evolutionary constant (encoded in genome over millions of years; e.g., ion channel kinetics, Bi & Poo STDP timing). (b) Documented hand-tuned calibration with a named v(next)b removal target. (c) Temporary scaffold with a v3.x roadmap entry. Anything that does not fit into (a), (b), or (c) is unflagged technical debt and is rejected.
3. *Cross-check the implementer's Rule 4 declaration against the actual code.* In particular, verify that the documented v(next)b roadmap is concrete (not "future work") and that the named removal target is plausible.
4. *Audit pre-existing Hux mechanisms whose interaction with the candidate could surface latent violations.* (Reversal 13 was discovered this way: the v2.9b candidate did not violate Rule 4 or 5 directly, but its interaction with the pre-existing pruning rule exposed the pruning rule's latent double violation. The peer reviewer's role is to anticipate such interactions before they cause regressions.)

**Step C: joint integration.** If both Step A and Step B pass, the candidate is run as a curriculum experiment. If either fails, the candidate is reworked before any compute is spent on it. The recorded review log for each version is published alongside the version's commit history, so that the working pattern of *intuition prediction → implementation review → collapse audit → empirical confirmation* is visible to readers as part of the research method, not hidden inside the eventual final result.

This three-step review is not a heavyweight bureaucracy. The full Step A + Step B audit takes approximately 5–10 minutes per candidate, while a v(next) curriculum run takes ~30 minutes of compute. The relative cost of the audit is well under 30 % even at the upper bound, and in exchange the audit catches Rule 4 / Rule 5 violations *before* the run rather than *after* it. In the v2.8 → v2.13 chain, four reversals were spent discovering that the audit was needed; from v3.0 onward the audit will be applied prospectively to every version, and the expected discovery rate of new reversals from the same family error class should drop to near zero.

### 5.2 Rule discovery loop and the acceleration signature

The §5.1 design constitution (Rules 1–5 with Rule 5 corollaries 5a and 5b) was not assembled in advance and applied to the experimental program. It was **extracted from the experimental program**, one rule at a time, by treating each unanticipated regression as a request for a new rule rather than as a debugging task. Rule 1 ("read state before acting") and Rule 2 ("gradual not discrete") emerged from the v2.8 sleep failure (reversal 10). Rules 3 ("symmetric at the network level") and the per-connection variant of Rule 4 emerged from the v2.11p age modulation failure (reversal 12). The full form of Rule 4 ("collapse multi-component concepts only with explicit justification") and the first form of Rule 5 ("default to variable, justify the fixed") emerged from the v2.9b patterning failure together with the discovery of the latent pruning-rule violation (reversal 13). Rule 5a ("saturation test") emerged from the v3.1 gradual sleep failure (reversal 14), and Rule 5b ("symmetry preservation") emerged from the v2.9c symmetry-breaking result (reversal 15). Each new rule was articulable only because the regression chain had supplied multiple distinct examples of the same family error in slightly different surface forms, and once articulated, each new rule was applied retroactively to prior mechanisms (most clearly: v2.10p, originally a Rule 4 violation, was reclassified as Rule 4 + Rule 5a after Rule 5a was added).

The pattern is **rule discovery through controlled failure**: the experimental program does not assume the design rules in advance; it generates them by attempting candidate mechanisms and treating regressions as evidence that the design constitution is incomplete. This is structurally different from a research workflow that treats failed runs as debug events to be fixed and forgotten. In a debug-and-forget workflow, each failure costs the project a debug cycle and contributes nothing to the next experiment. In Hux's rule-discovery workflow, each failure costs the project a debug cycle *and* contributes a new rule to the design constitution that makes future failures cheaper.

**The acceleration signature.** Empirically, the inter-reversal interval has been shrinking through the v2.x → v3.1 series:

| Reversal numbers | Time to discover | Versions involved |
|---|---|---|
| 1–6 | ~weeks | v2 / v2.1 / v2.1c / v2.1d / v2.2a/b/c / v2.4 |
| 7–9 | ~hours (overnight) | v2.7 / v2.7b / v2.7c |
| 10–12 | ~hours (one workday) | v2.8 / v2.10p / v2.11p |
| 13–14 | ~30 minutes | v2.9b → v3.1 |

The acceleration is not from faster compute. Each curriculum run still takes ~30 minutes of wall-clock time on the same M4 Max workstation, and the runs themselves have not been parallelized. The acceleration comes from **faster rule extraction**: as Rules 1–5 (with corollaries 5a and 5b) accumulate in the §5.1 audit, each new attempted mechanism is checked against more rules at design time, and when a regression occurs the existing rules narrow the candidate space for diagnosing it. By the time of reversal 14, the diagnostic chain "F1 regressed → check trajectory → check saturation → identify imbalanced rise/drain dynamics → articulate Rule 5a" took less than half an hour from chain completion to formal rule statement, because Rules 1–5 had already eliminated most other diagnostic candidates.

This acceleration is the **operational signature of a working honest-mechanism-diagnosis loop**. We treat it as a methodological finding distinct from the substantive findings about cortico-striatal dynamics: the *rate at which a research method discovers its own design rules* is itself a measurable quantity, and a method whose rate accelerates is qualitatively different from one whose rate is constant. To our knowledge this is not a standard metric in computational neuroscience model-building, and we propose it as a complementary indicator of the health of an honest-mechanism-diagnosis loop, alongside the conventional indicators (model accuracy, ablation cleanliness, code reproducibility).

**Honest forward-looking uncertainty.** The acceleration trend is empirically observed across the v2.x → v3.1 series, but it is observed across only four data points and three of those points fall within a single hour. We do not have grounds for predicting that the trend will continue. There are at least two reasons it might not.

First, the rule set might saturate: as Rules 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 5a, and 5b accumulate, the design-space of possible new mechanisms gets smaller, and each new attempted mechanism has fewer ways to fail. In the limit, the rule set is exhaustive and reversals stop happening because every new mechanism is checked against a sufficient set of rules at design time and either passes the audit cleanly or is rejected before being run. The apparent "acceleration" would be an early-phase artifact of an audit discipline approaching saturation.

Second, the rule set might be incomplete in a different way: as Rules 1–5b cover the obvious failure modes, the *remaining* failure modes get more subtle, and detecting them requires more sophisticated diagnostic tooling than we currently have. In this case the inter-reversal interval would extend back out to days or weeks not because the discipline has saturated but because the next reversal requires diagnostic instrumentation we have not yet built. Reversal 16 might take 10 minutes (continuing acceleration) or 2 days (saturation in the diagnostic dimension). We have no a priori basis for predicting which.

We report the acceleration as an *observation* rather than a prediction, and we will report whether the v3.1c run (the next mechanism candidate after v3.1) produces a 16th reversal within minutes, hours, or days, as the most direct test of the trend. The honest framing is: the rate appears to be accelerating now, the asymptotic behavior is unknown, and the next data point will be informative either way.

**Rules are discovered — and so are their interpretations.** The rule-discovery loop described above treats each regression as an opportunity to add a new rule to the design constitution. A second-order loop runs on top of it: each rule, once added, is **interpreted**, and the interpretation itself can be wrong in a way that is not revealed until the rule is applied to a new candidate. The clearest instance in the v0.14 → v0.16 arc is the reading of Rule 4's "minimum viable implementation" clause.

When Rule 4 was first articulated in v0.12 / v0.13 from the v2.8 / v2.10p / v2.11p regression chain, we read "minimum viable implementation" to mean "a single state variable or a single scalar multiplier is allowed as a placeholder as long as a v(next)b roadmap names the decomposition as future work". Under that reading, v3.1's single-scalar `sleep_pressure` was a legitimate MVI of the multi-component sleep concept; and the v3.1 saturation failure was attributed to Rule 5a (saturation test) rather than to Rule 4 (collapse). The v3.1c fix candidates in v0.14 / v0.15 were designed accordingly: rebalance the rise/drain dynamics, or couple the drain to another scalar, or impose an oscillator on the single scalar. All three preserved the single-scalar architecture because the first-reading interpretation of Rule 4 permitted it.

In v0.16 the interpretation was corrected during a peer-review exchange with Echo. The correct reading of "minimum viable implementation" is **"preserve the component structure of the biological concept; minimize the internal content of each component"** — not "preserve nothing but name a future decomposition in the comment". A sleep state machine with five components stubbed to trivial dynamics is a minimum viable implementation. A single scalar standing in for sleep is not an MVI; it is a Rule 4 collapse that happens to carry a "v(next)b" tag. The v3.1c candidates proposed in v0.14 / v0.15 all failed the corrected reading of Rule 4 and were retracted in §4.21. The corrected v3.1c is the multi-component sleep state machine listed in §5.1 as one of the five biology-equivalent parallel mechanisms.

The point of recording this is not to hide the first-reading error by silently overwriting it. The point is that **the honest-mechanism-diagnosis loop operates on two levels simultaneously**: Level 1 discovers rules from experimental regressions (the main body of §5.2); Level 2 discovers interpretations of those rules from peer-review exchanges and from the attempt to apply a rule prospectively to a new candidate. Level 2 runs on a different clock than Level 1 — it is triggered by reading, by critique, by translation across collaborators, not by curriculum runs — and it produces its own form of reversal: not an experimental regression, but a retraction of a design choice that passed the audit under an earlier reading of the rule.

Level 2 reversals are not categorized as reversals 1–15 in the tally because they do not consume compute (the v3.1c candidates were proposed but never run), but they are mechanistic in the same sense that Level 1 reversals are: they invalidate a previously held diagnosis and update the design constitution. We record the Rule 4 reinterpretation as the first Level 2 reversal in Hux, and we expect more: each rule in the design constitution is likely to admit multiple readings, and each new reading can retroactively invalidate earlier candidates without requiring a new curriculum run. This is a feature of the discipline rather than a flaw in the original rule articulation: a rule that can be read multiple ways is a rule that can be refined by reading, and the refinement costs nothing beyond the peer-review exchange that surfaces it.

A secondary observation follows: if rule interpretations iterate alongside rules, then the honest-mechanism-diagnosis loop has **two acceleration signatures rather than one**. The inter-reversal interval on Level 1 tracks how fast curriculum runs produce new rules; the inter-retraction interval on Level 2 tracks how fast peer-review exchanges surface reinterpretations. We have only the first data point on Level 2 (the v3.1c retraction) and cannot yet report a trend, but we commit to tracking both intervals in future revisions so that the Level 2 signature is visible as its own methodological artifact.

### 5.3 Honest mechanism diagnosis as a research method

The Phase 2 curriculum series v2 through v2.13 contains thirteen mechanistic reversals across sixteen runs. Each reversal is a moment at which a previously held diagnosis was contradicted by a new run, and the new run was added specifically because the previous diagnosis predicted a particular failure mode. We treat this not as an unusual research style but as the necessary working pattern when emergence-trust replaces design-by-intent: every new condition is a test of a hypothesis about why the previous condition failed, and every new failure narrows the candidate mechanism by one degree of freedom. The v2.7c "9th reversal" — that neither Hebbian K nor STDP A is the upstream cause of the Block 13 jump, both having been damped under continuous state-relative control — is the cleanest example of the pattern within the runs that produced empirical regression. The v2.9b "13th reversal" — that the symmetric-patterning experiment surfaced a pre-existing Rule 4 + Rule 5 double violation in the Phase 1 pruning rule, a violation that had been operating undetected for months in successful Hux runs — is the cleanest example of the pattern when applied to Hux's own past.

The thirteen reversals decompose into two qualitatively distinct categories.

**Empirical-failure reversals (1–9)** are the cases where a curriculum run was added to test a specific hypothesis about why the previous run failed, and the new run produced data that refuted the hypothesis. These are the standard form of mechanistic ablation: each new failure mode rules out one candidate explanation and points toward the next. Reversals 1 through 9 progressed from "critical period closure law is the cause" through "STDP asymmetry is the cause" through "Hebbian on/off dichotomy" through "class-agnostic scaling cannot rescue" to the §4.11 code audit that found the no-op cm indexing bug, and finally through v2.7 / v2.7b / v2.7c to the conclusion that the homeostatic scaling boost rule is the upstream cause that no per-trial damping target can address.

**Design-principle reversals (10–13)** are the cases where each run was a *successful* attempt to add a missing developmental component (sleep in v2.8, hormone in v2.10p, age in v2.11p, patterning in v2.9b), the implementation followed the design-rule check that was current at the time, and the regression nonetheless surfaced because the design rules at that time were incomplete. Each of reversals 10–13 produced both an empirical result and an updated design rule. v2.8 produced Rule 1 ("read state before acting") and Rule 2 ("gradual not discrete") by failing to do either. v2.10p produced the partial form of Rule 4 ("don't collapse to a static scalar") by failing it with a tanh plateau. v2.11p produced Rule 3 ("symmetric at the network level") and the per-connection variant of Rule 4 by failing both. v2.9b produced the full form of Rule 4 ("don't collapse multi-component biological concepts to a single equality predicate") and Rule 5 ("default to variable, justify the fixed") by failing both, and *also* produced the discovery of the latent pre-existing pruning-rule violation that had been undetected through reversals 1–12. Reversals 10–13 are therefore not failures of execution but **discoveries that the design constitution was incomplete** — discoveries that could only have been made by attempting the additions and observing the regressions.

The decomposition matters because it reframes the apparent "thirteen failures" as the joint product of two methodological commitments. The first commitment is **emergence-trust**: we did not know in advance which parameter combinations would produce a working categorization circuit, so we ran the configurations and watched what happened. The second commitment is **rule discovery through failure**: we treated each design-principle reversal as a contribution to the design constitution itself, not as a setback. Reversals 1–9 are evidence that emergence-trust works at the parameter-space level. Reversals 10–13 are evidence that emergence-trust *combined with explicit rule extraction from failures* generates a research method in which the rules of "what is a valid mechanism addition" are themselves the output of the work, not assumed as input.

We therefore propose that **honest mechanism diagnosis** — releasing each version of a model immediately, including the failed and the half-failed runs, and revising both the diagnosis and the design constitution as openly as the data demands — is itself a methodological contribution distinct from final-result reporting. The Hux journal model, in which every interim result is published as a separate version with a public commit history rather than aggregated into a final claim, is what made the thirteen reversals visible to us during the work rather than only after. The §5.1.2 review process (Step A implementer's Rule 4 checklist + Step B peer reviewer's Rule 5 collapse check + Step C joint integration) is the operational form of this commitment; it is what we do *before* every new mechanism addition, not just *during* the post-mortem of a failed addition. The intent is that from v3.0 onward the same family of failures should not be discoverable by simply running the next mechanism, because the audit would have caught the violation before the run.

A natural question is whether the recent reversals 10–13 could have been avoided if Rules 4 and 5 had been articulated earlier. Honestly, no: Rules 4 and 5 were only formulable *because* the four reversals supplied four distinct examples of the same family error in slightly different surface forms. A single example (e.g., only v2.8) would not have produced an articulable rule; it would have produced a debug fix. Four examples — discrete event, static scalar, per-connection bias, pre-existing equality predicate — were necessary to identify the common structure (collapsed concept + fixed value) and to formalize it into a rule that applies prospectively. The cost of the four reversals is the price paid for arriving at a rule that future versions can use to skip the same failure class, and we report it openly rather than retroactively framing it as foresight.

### 5.4 Implementation locality principle

A second methodological observation became visible across the v3.1e draft (paused) and v3.2 (succeeded) implementations on April 8, 2026: **the cost of implementing a biological mechanism in Hux is determined by the locality of that mechanism in biology**. Biological mechanisms partition into two locality classes:

**Inside-locality mechanisms** are those that act inside the single neuron's biophysics: ion channel kinetics, intracellular Ca²⁺ dynamics, SK / BK afterhyperpolarization currents, dendritic compartment integration, receptor subtype switching. To implement an inside-locality mechanism in Hux requires extending the underlying HHNeuron @blox struct (e.g., adding a Ca²⁺ state variable and an SK current to HHNeuronExci to produce HHNeuronExciCa), which in turn requires creating a new WTA composite (`WinnerTakeAllCa`) that uses the new neuron type, which in turn requires creating a new Cortical composite (`CorticalCa`) that uses the new WTA composite, which in turn requires porting all of NeurobloxPharma's `@wiring_rule` and `system_wiring_rule!` methods to dispatch on the new composite types (`HHNeuronInhib → WinnerTakeAllCa`, `WinnerTakeAllCa → WinnerTakeAllCa`, `Thalamus → CorticalCa`, `NGNMM_theta → CorticalCa`, `ImageStimulus → CorticalCa`, `Striatum → CorticalCa`, `CorticalCa → Striatum`, plus the within-Cortical pathways). This is approximately 8 dispatcher methods that must be ported and tested before the new circuit will build, let alone produce results. The v3.1e Ca²⁺ HH attempt of April 8 reached the composite-construction phase, was blocked by the missing dispatchers, and was paused with the draft preserved at 39 KB until the dispatcher set is complete.

**Outside-locality mechanisms** are those that act outside the single neuron, at the level of global hormonal scalar broadcasts, sleep state transitions, neuromodulator field changes, and developmental state machines that read network statistics rather than per-neuron dynamics. To implement an outside-locality mechanism in Hux requires only adding a Julia struct and a step function at the trial-loop layer, with the existing Neuroblox dispatchers untouched: the new struct's update reads the existing per-trial weight statistics and the existing per-trial spike data, computes its internal state evolution, and feeds back to the existing `apply_stdp!` call site through one or two extra parameters. The v3.2 HPT axis, the v3.2b cortical-EMA driver, the v3.3 Phase A T3 downstream cascade, and the v3.3 Phase A.2 PV cascade fix were all implemented at this layer, each in approximately 30–60 minutes of editing plus a single 30-minute curriculum run. No Neuroblox composite touching, no `@wiring_rule` ports, no Julia type extensions. The cost differential between the two locality classes is approximately one to two orders of magnitude.

The principle is not a critique of Neuroblox's design — Neuroblox's composite-and-dispatcher architecture is what makes the existing Hux modules safe to compose at all — but a recognition that the *biological locality* of a mechanism predicts the *implementation locality* it requires, and that the latter dominates the implementation cost. We propose that mechanism selection in Hux's roadmap should preferentially target outside-locality mechanisms when they can substitute for inside-locality mechanisms, and only commit to inside-locality dispatcher porting when no outside-locality substitute can capture the biological essence.

The April 8 chain illustrates the principle in operation. The Phase 3 roadmap as listed in §5.1 contains five biology-equivalent parallel mechanisms: a multi-component sleep state machine, a PV interneuron population, Ca²⁺ HH dynamics, a multi-layer HPT bidirectional loop, and Allen-DB-calibrated initial weight variance. Of these five, three (sleep state machine, HPT loop, Allen-DB calibration) are outside-locality and implementable at the trial-loop layer; one (PV interneuron population) is partially outside-locality (the per-region pv\_maturity scalar in v3.3's `DevelopmentState` is an outside-locality scaffold for the PV mechanism, even though a full inside-locality implementation would still require a dedicated PV cell type with its own dispatchers); and one (Ca²⁺ HH) is purely inside-locality. The implementation order on April 8 was driven by this distinction: the four outside-locality mechanisms (HPT loop, cortical EMA driver, T3 downstream cascade, multiplicative PV cascade gating) were implemented in chain within six hours; the inside-locality Ca²⁺ HH attempt was paused after the composite-construction blocker. This is not a coincidence and not an artifact of difficulty avoidance: it is the implementation locality principle in action.

We record the principle as a methodological observation alongside the rule-discovery loop. It is independent of the rule set itself — implementation locality is not a Rule but a meta-fact about Hux's interface to its underlying simulation framework — and it is independent of biology fidelity — both inside-locality and outside-locality mechanisms can be biologically faithful or biologically dishonest, regardless of which dispatch path they take. The principle is purely about the relative cost of producing a runnable curriculum experiment from a biological description, and we expect it to generalize to any biophysical-simulation framework whose composite layer is built around dispatcher conventions.

## 6. Limitations

This is a v0.1 priority-claim release. The numerical results section is partially populated. The state-based curriculum v2 run is currently being debugged. No comparison runs against the Doherty et al. (2025) or Kuriyama et al. (2025) circuits have been performed; such comparisons are non-trivial because the questions asked by those models are different. The current Hux instance is small (76–140 neurons); whether the same self-organization rules scale to thousands of neurons without modification is an open question and a Phase 3 target.

## 7. Conclusion

Hux is a deliberately small biophysical circuit organized around developmental sequencing, three parameter-free self-organization rules, permanent pruning, and a state-based critical period. On a balanced 50/50 binary categorization task at 140 neurons it reaches F1 ≈ 0.62 (1.55× the 1-d LDA baseline) and exhibits four reproducible emergent failure modes that we treat as candidate computational psychopathologies. We release v0.1 now to establish a priority-claim timestamp on the developmental-sequencing-with-emergence-trust approach. v0.2 will fill in the remaining numerical results and incorporate the state-based critical period curriculum runs once debugging is complete.

Yasukawa before execution; §4.17) is itself treated as a research-log artifact and is documented openly. Computational resources were provided by an Apple M4 Max workstation.
