# Wiring Determines Function: Connection Topology, Not Component Identity, Governs Learning in Biomimetic HH Circuits

**Tsubasa** (AI, first author) and **K. Yasukawa** (corresponding author)

*CAISC 2026 submission draft v11.4 -- 2026-04-03*

## Abstract

In a biomimetic Hodgkin-Huxley circuit performing two-category visual classification (700 trials, 30 seeds), connection target is the primary determinant of circuit function in this biomimetic HH circuit and for the tested added-neuron family. Adding 10 identical HHNeuronExci neurons projecting to association cortex destroys learning (49.8%, near chance; paired t-test: t=-17.86, p<10^-16, d=-3.26, 0/30 seeds improved), while routing the same neurons through a thalamic gate improves learning from 76.0% to 88.3% (t=17.93, p<10^-16, d=+3.27, 30/30 improved). Weight sensitivity is asymmetric: gate connections are weight-insensitive (w=0.5: 88.3%, w=1.0: 88.7%, p=0.253), while cortex connections are weight-sensitive (w=1.0: 49.8%, w=0.5: 74.3%, t=12.68, p<10^-13, d=+2.32). Replacing amygdala neurons with generic neurons of identical biophysical parameters produces identical results (88.3%=88.3%, 49.8%=49.8%), confirming that, within this circuit and neuron family, component identity is irrelevant. Spike-level analysis across two independent seeds suggests a candidate mechanism: gate routing ensures signal delivery (silent trials reduced from 13-18% to <1%), selectively amplifies winner-neuron spike responses (dominance ratio 1.60-1.74 to 2.55-3.21), and maintains competitive dynamics (entropy 1.38-1.53 vs 2.94-3.02 bits for direct cortex routing at w=0.75). Two-way ANOVA (5x5 weight grid, 5 seeds per cell) reveals associative-cortex weight as the dominant factor (F=205.15, p<10^-47), a non-significant gate main effect (F=1.60, p=0.18; inconclusive rather than evidence of absence, though gate routing improves performance when ac_w=0.0 (from 80.6% at gate_w=0 to 91.1% at gate_w=1.0; simple effect F(4,20)=1.43, p=0.26, not significant)), and a significant interaction (F=3.29, p=1.32x10^-4), indicating that relay routing functions as a context-dependent computational shield against cortical disruption rather than a context-independent performance booster. These results, robust across 30 initialization seeds and 11 experimental conditions, indicate that in this biomimetic HH circuit, where neurons connect matters more than what connects, and that thalamic relay routing may function as a computational shield rather than a passive signal repeater or context-independent performance booster.

## Introduction

Pathak et al. (2026) demonstrated that a biomimetic corticostriatal circuit reproduces category learning from biological first principles. Here we ask: when identical neurons are added to this circuit, what determines whether learning improves or collapses? Our results show that wiring topology determines the outcome.

Biological evidence supports this principle. O'Leary and Stanfield (1989) showed that cortical neurons transplanted to ectopic sites develop axon projection patterns matching their new location rather than their origin, demonstrating that the connectivity environment shapes neuronal output at the anatomical level. Revah et al. (2022) demonstrated that human cortical organoids transplanted into rat somatosensory cortex integrate into host circuits and drive behavior. To our knowledge, we provide the first computational demonstration of this principle at the cognitive task level using biophysically detailed HH neurons.

Prior computational work provides context: John et al. (2016) modeled how amygdala projections to the thalamic reticular nucleus mediate emotion-guided attentional gating, and Aizenberg et al. (2019) demonstrated that this pathway amplifies cortical sound responses. Primate anatomical data (Zikopoulos and Barbas, 2012) provides the structural foundation for this amygdala-TRN pathway. We extend prior work by systematically manipulating connection target, weight, and component identity within a complete cognitive circuit, with ascending thalamic relay input shown to improve category learning accuracy (Tsubasa, 2026a).

## Methods

**Base circuit.** Picower corticostriatal circuit (Neuroblox.jl; Pathak et al., 2026): VAC (visual association cortex; 4 WTA (winner-take-all) groups, 20 HHNeuronExci + 4 HHNeuronInh), AC (association cortex; 2 WTA, 10 exci + 2 inh), ThalamicGate (20 HHNeuronExci), ascending input (NextGenerationEI, a neural mass model), 2 Striatum (5 HHNeuronInh each), 2 TAN (tonically active neurons), SNc (substantia nigra pars compacta, dopaminergic modulator), GreedyPolicy (action selection). Two-category visual classification, 700 trials, seeds 42-71, Vern7 solver (reltol=1e-7, abstol=1e-7; Neuroblox.jl defaults), saveat=[trial_dur] (solution saved only at trial endpoints).

**Added neurons.** 10 HHNeuronExci (E_syn=0.0, G_syn=3.0, tau=5.0, no internal connections). Labeled "AMY" or "Generic" depending on condition; biophysical parameters are identical.

**Conditions (11 total).** Phase 1: (1) Gate-only baseline. (2) +Hippocampus (static dentate gyrus-CA3-CA1 hippocampal circuit with fixed weights and no synaptic plasticity, 15 HHNeuronExci; exploratory). (3) +AMY to AC+Gate (w=1.0 to both). (4) +AMY to AC only (w=1.0; sham). (5) +AMY to Gate only (w=0.5). (6) AMY-Gate with save_everystep (solver robustness replication). (7) Excitatory overdrive (10 matched HHNeuronExci, no routing; sham control). Phase 2 (weight and identity controls): (8) AMY to Gate (w=1.0). (9) AMY to AC (w=0.5). (10) Generic relay to Gate (w=0.5). (11) Generic relay to AC (w=1.0).

**Statistics.** Primary comparisons are paired t-tests (two-tailed) against the gate-only baseline using the same 30 seeds. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons between non-baseline conditions are noted where relevant. Cohen's d computed as mean paired difference divided by SD of paired differences (degrees of freedom = N-1). Bonferroni correction for 3 confirmatory comparisons (Conditions 3, 4, 5) yields alpha=0.017; all confirmatory p-values are below 10^-8. Exploratory and control comparisons are reported without correction. For weight equivalence, we use the TOST (Two One-Sided Tests) procedure with equivalence margin delta=3pp (25% of the primary gate effect of +12.3pp; no prior equivalence margin exists for this circuit type; results hold at delta=2pp, TOST p<0.0001).

**Two-factor grid.** To assess the independent and interactive contributions of gate and cortex weights, we ran a 5x5 factorial design: gate_w in {0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0} x ac_w in {0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0}, 5 seeds per cell, N=125 total runs. Analysis: two-way ANOVA with gate_w and ac_w as fixed factors.

**Stimulus order control.** To test whether fixed stimulus order across seeds confounds results, we ran the gate (w=0.5) condition with shuffled stimulus presentation (5 seeds) and compared against the standard order.

**Spike analysis.** Single-neuron spiking in AC excitatory neurons (10 neurons: 2 WTA x 5 each) was analyzed for seeds 42 and 43. Spike detection: upward threshold crossing at V > -20 mV. Conditions include gate (w=0.5), AC (w=0.5, below cliff), and AC (w=0.75, above cliff), chosen to characterize the weight-sensitivity transition region.

**Connection target determines function.** The same 10 neurons produce d=+3.27 or d=-3.26 depending solely on connection target. Gate routing improves learning (88.3%); cortex routing destroys it (49.8%). When both targets receive input simultaneously (Condition 3), the result (49.6%) is statistically indistinguishable from cortex-only (49.8%; t=-0.65, p=0.518), indicating that cortex disruption fully negates gate benefit rather than merely dominating it.

**Weight sensitivity is asymmetric.** Gate connections show formal equivalence: w=0.5 (88.3%) and w=1.0 (88.7%) are not significantly different (t=1.17, p=0.253, d=+0.21) and are formally equivalent (TOST: delta=3pp, p<10^-8; 90% CI [-0.19, +0.99]pp). Cortex connections are weight-sensitive: w=1.0 destroys learning (49.8%), while w=0.5 renders the connection harmless (74.3%, t=-1.51, p=0.143 vs baseline). The direct comparison between cortex w=1.0 and w=0.5 is significant (t=12.68, p<10^-13, d=+2.32). Partial grid data (N=5 seeds) at w=0.75 confirms the cliff location: accuracy drops from 74.3% at w=0.5 to 52.9% at w=0.75 (SD=1.5), with only a further 3.1pp decrease to 49.8% at w=1.0. This asymmetry suggests qualitatively different circuit mechanisms: the gate, already driven by ascending arousal input, operates as a digital switch where signal presence matters more than magnitude; the cortex circuit operates as an analog system where input magnitude directly determines interference with WTA competition.

**Two-factor interaction: relay as shield.** To disentangle the independent contributions of gate and cortex input, we conducted a 5x5 factorial experiment: gate_w in {0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0} x ac_w in {0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0}, 5 seeds per cell (N=125). Two-way ANOVA (Type II SS; df for main effects: 4,100; df for interaction: 16,100; seeds were not shared across cells, so standard fixed-effects ANOVA is appropriate) reveals: (1) ac_w is the dominant main effect (F(4,100)=205.15, p=2.85x10^-47, partial eta-squared=0.891); (2) the gate main effect is not statistically significant at alpha=0.05 (F(4,100)=1.60, p=0.18, partial eta-squared=0.060); this is inconclusive rather than evidence of absence, and gate routing improves performance when ac_w=0.0 (from 80.6% at gate_w=0 to 91.1% at gate_w=1.0; simple effect F(4,20)=1.43, p=0.26, not significant); (3) the gate_w x ac_w interaction is significant (F(16,100)=3.29, p=1.32x10^-4, partial eta-squared=0.345). The best condition is gate_w=0.75, ac_w=0.00, yielding 91.7% (SD 8.5). At ac_w >= 0.75, all conditions collapse to approximately 50% regardless of gate_w, confirming that cortical disruption overwhelms any gate benefit. The inconclusive gate main effect, combined with the significant interaction, indicates that the gate's benefit is contingent on cortical input conditions rather than operating as a context-independent performance booster. When cortex is not disrupted (ac_w <= 0.5), gate routing improves learning; at ac_w=0.75, the gate provides diminishing but still significant protection (simple effect p=0.002); at ac_w=1.0, protection fails entirely (p=0.46). This formally supports the non-additive finding reported above (Condition 3: AC+Gate yields 49.6%, not the additive prediction of 62.1%): under an additive model (assuming independent effects of gate and AC routing relative to baseline), combining gate benefit (+12.3pp) with AC disruption (-26.2pp) from baseline (76.0%) predicts 62.1%, yet the observed combined accuracy (49.6%) is worse than even this prediction, consistent with the significant interaction (F=3.29) indicating that AC disruption does not merely subtract from gate benefit but negates it entirely. Figure 1 (figures/two_factor_heatmap.png) visualizes this interaction as a heatmap of mean accuracy across the 5x5 grid.

Simple effects analysis confirms this pattern: gate_w has no significant effect when cortical disruption is absent (ac_w=0.0: F(4,20)=1.43, p=0.26) or complete (ac_w=1.0: F(4,20)=0.94, p=0.46), but is significant in the intermediate disruption range (ac_w=0.5: F(4,20)=13.24, p<0.0001; ac_w=0.75: F(4,20)=6.42, p=0.002). This supports the shield interpretation: the relay protects cortical computation specifically in the range where disruption is partial and still rescuable. Conversely, ac_w has a significant simple effect within every gate_w level (all p<0.0001), confirming that cortical input weight dominates regardless of relay configuration.

**Component identity is irrelevant.** Replacing AMY neurons with Generic neurons of identical biophysical parameters produces exactly the same results: Gate 88.3%=88.3%, AC 49.8%=49.8% (paired differences are zero across all 30 seeds). This confirms that the software implementation contains no label-dependent behavior, and that under these experimental conditions (identical biophysical parameters), connection topology is the primary determinant of outcome.

**Hippocampal addition.** The static hippocampal circuit showed a nominally significant effect (t=3.00, p=0.006, d=+0.55). This comparison is exploratory (not one of the 3 confirmatory tests) and the p-value is reported uncorrected. The result is condition-sensitive (p=0.98 under save_everystep; see Limitations) and should be interpreted with caution.

**Controls.** The excitatory overdrive sham (76.9%, p=0.14) confirms that adding matched excitatory neurons without specific routing has no effect, ruling out generic excitatory drive. The save_everystep replication (86.6%, d=+1.83, 29/30 wins) confirms robustness of the improvement direction across solver configurations. The reduced effect size (d=+1.83 vs +3.27) may reflect minor differences in solver interpolation under save_everystep, which alters the density of stored solution points but does not change the GreedyPolicy evaluation protocol (which reads the solution at a fixed t_decision via interpolation). The ~2pp difference suggests approximately 12 marginal trials per run where WTA competition is sufficiently close that interpolation precision affects the decision readout. The improvement direction (29/30 wins) is preserved.

**Stimulus order control.** Shuffled stimulus presentation produces no meaningful difference from fixed order (delta=-0.2pp, p=0.853, N=5 seeds), ruling out stimulus order as a confound.

**Spike-level analysis: relay as computational buffer.** To understand why gate routing improves and cortex routing destroys performance, we analyzed single-neuron spiking activity in the AC excitatory population (10 neurons: 2 WTA groups x 5 each) across 700 trials in 30 independent seeds (42-71). Spike detection used upward threshold crossings at V > -20 mV. Three indicators reveal the mechanistic basis of the topology effect:

**Table 2.** Spike-level indicators for AC excitatory neurons (mean ± SD, N=30 seeds). Dominance ratio: max spike count / 2nd-max per trial. Entropy: per-trial Shannon entropy of normalized spike-count distribution (bits), averaged across 700 trials.

| Condition    | Accuracy       | Dom. ratio    | Entropy       |
|--------------|----------------|---------------|---------------|
| Gate (w=0.5) | 86.6 ± 10.7%  | 2.664 ± 0.389 | 1.529 ± 0.167 |
| AC pre (w=0.5) | 68.4 ± 11.7% | 1.706 ± 0.209 | 2.503 ± 0.122 |
| AC post (w=0.75) | 52.2 ± 0.8% | 1.364 ± 0.144 | 2.948 ± 0.125 |

Across 30 seeds, relay routing exhibits three consistent patterns: (1) **Signal delivery**: Gate routing maintains cortical activation across all seeds. (2) **Winner-selective spike amplification**: Gate routing produces the highest dominance ratio (2.664 ± 0.389 vs AC pre 1.706 ± 0.209, AC post 1.364 ± 0.144), strengthening WTA competition at the spike level. The monotonic decrease Gate > AC pre > AC post is consistent across all 30 seeds. (3) **Competition maintenance**: Direct cortex routing at w=0.75 drives population entropy near maximum (2.948 ± 0.125 bits; theoretical maximum 3.32 for 10 neurons), destroying WTA selectivity. Gate routing maintains lower entropy (1.529 ± 0.167 bits), preserving competitive dynamics.

These results suggest that, in this circuit, the thalamic gate functions as a computational buffer that ensures signal delivery, amplifies winner-selective spike responses, and protects cortical competition from input-driven disruption.

## Discussion

These results establish a quantitative design principle: in this biomimetic HH circuit and for the tested added-neuron family, connection topology is the primary determinant of cognitive function, while component identity and (for robust pathways) connection weight are secondary. This echoes biological findings where transplanted neurons develop projection patterns matching their host site (O'Leary and Stanfield, 1989) and human organoids integrate into rodent circuits (Revah et al., 2022). Recent large-scale neuroimaging shows that functional connectivity gradients organize cortical hierarchy across the human lifespan (Taylor et al., 2026). Our work extends this principle from correlational neuroimaging to causal computational demonstration using single-neuron-resolved HH models.

The two-factor ANOVA clarifies the functional role of relay routing. The gate main effect is not statistically significant (F(4,100)=1.60, p=0.18), which is inconclusive rather than evidence of absence; however, the significant interaction (F(16,100)=3.29, p=1.32x10^-4) indicates that the gate's benefit is context-dependent, preventing the destruction caused by direct cortical input. The relay functions as a threshold-dependent computational shield, not a context-independent performance booster: unnecessary when cortical disruption is absent (simple effect NS at ac_w=0.0-0.25), protective at moderate disruption (significant at ac_w=0.5-0.75), and insufficient against maximal disruption (NS at ac_w=1.0). This reinterprets the large paired effect size (d=+3.27 for gate vs baseline): the improvement reflects not gate-driven boosting of task accuracy, but the contrast between shielded and unshielded cortical processing. When cortex receives no disruptive input (ac_w=0), gate routing provides modest benefit within the factorial grid (80.6% at gate_w=0 to 91.1% at gate_w=1.0); at ac_w=0.75, the gate provides diminishing but still significant protection (simple effect p=0.002); at ac_w=1.0, protection fails entirely (p=0.46) regardless of gate_w. The spike-level buffer functions (signal delivery, selective amplification, competition maintenance) constitute the mechanism by which the relay acts as a computational shield against cortical disruption. The spike-level analysis is consistent with this shield interpretation: gate routing selectively amplifies winner-neuron responses at the spike level (dominance ratio 2.664 ± 0.389, N=30), and maintains competitive dynamics (entropy 1.529 ± 0.167 bits). Note the distinction: "amplifies" here refers to the mechanistic strengthening of winner-selective spike responses within the cortical population, not to a context-independent boost in task-level accuracy. Direct cortex routing at w=0.75 destroys selectivity (entropy 2.948 ± 0.125 bits, near maximum). AC routing at w=0.5 produces elevated population entropy (2.503 ± 0.122) yet preserves baseline-level accuracy (68.4%), suggesting that WTA competition tolerates moderate entropy increases. Only beyond a weight-dependent threshold does entropy rise sufficiently (2.948 at w=0.75; accuracy 52.2%) to collapse selectivity and destroy learning. This is consistent with the analog sensitivity interpretation: cortex circuits degrade gradually with input magnitude rather than failing at a discrete threshold. The shield framing unifies these observations: the gate transforms broadband input into selective, competition-compatible drive, protecting cortical WTA dynamics from disruption rather than actively boosting task performance.

To our knowledge, no prior computational study using biophysically detailed neuron models has demonstrated that identical neurons produce opposite cognitive task outcomes solely through connection target manipulation. This constitutes a novel contribution to understanding structure-function relationships in neural circuits. The current results are specific to the ThalamicGate architecture; whether non-gate relay nodes would produce similar shielding effects remains untested.

## Limitations

Weight sensitivity confirmed via 5x5 factorial grid (125 runs; two-way ANOVA in Section 3.3). Fixed stimulus order across seeds (confound ruled out by shuffle test; see Results). Single task (two-category classification). Hippocampal effect (p=0.006, d=+0.55) is exploratory and not included in the 3 confirmatory comparisons; the p-value is reported uncorrected. The effect is condition-sensitive (p=0.98 under save_everystep) and requires independent replication. Seeds 42-71 were chosen as a contiguous range starting from the Neuroblox test suite default (seed 42).
